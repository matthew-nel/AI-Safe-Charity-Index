# Purpose

This document defines a practical maturity model for how charities adopt and govern AI.

The model helps charities understand:

- where they are now
- what risks are most relevant
- what good practice looks like at their stage
- what to prioritise next
- how governance should scale with complexity

The model is designed first for small and medium-sized charities.

Larger and more complex organisations can use the same structure with deeper controls.

# Core Principle

> Start with the smallest safe step, then add governance as use becomes more important, more widespread or more risky.

The maturity model should not make small charities feel inadequate.

It should help them progress proportionately.

# Maturity Levels

Use five levels.

## Level 0 — Unaware or Unmanaged

Typical signs:

- staff may already be using AI informally
- no one owns AI
- no policy exists
- no approved-tools list exists
- personal accounts may be used
- risks are not discussed
- trustees may be unaware of current use

Main risks:

- accidental data disclosure
- inconsistent practice
- unreviewed outputs
- shadow AI
- safeguarding concerns
- reputational harm

Priority actions:

1. Find out what tools staff are using.
2. Share the Before You Paste guidance.
3. Agree a short do-not-paste rule.
4. Name one responsible person.
5. Ask staff to report current use.

Minimum evidence of progress:

- basic staff conversation
- named owner
- simple first rules
- initial list of tools in use

## Level 1 — Aware and Exploring

Typical signs:

- staff are experimenting
- leadership knows AI is being used
- low-risk tasks are common
- some informal guidance exists
- confidence varies
- no consistent tool approval process

Main risks:

- inconsistent judgement
- confusion about personal data
- poor output review
- personal versus organisational account issues
- unclear escalation

Priority actions:

1. Create a simple AI policy.
2. Create an approved-tools list.
3. Train staff on Green, Amber and Red use.
4. Introduce a basic experiment log.
5. Define who answers questions.

Minimum evidence of progress:

- policy approved
- tools listed
- staff briefed
- questions routed to a named person
- low-risk uses documented

## Level 2 — Controlled and Consistent

Typical signs:

- AI policy is active
- approved tools are in place
- staff training exists
- some use cases are documented
- managers understand escalation
- risky uses are paused or reviewed

Main risks:

- gaps between teams
- incomplete supplier checks
- weak evidence trails
- inconsistent review of outputs
- unlogged experiments

Priority actions:

1. Maintain an AI-use register.
2. Review new tools before approval.
3. Add role-based guidance.
4. Review incidents and near misses.
5. Report material risks to leadership.
6. Review policy quarterly.

Minimum evidence of progress:

- current registers
- regular review
- documented tool assessment
- role-based guidance
- issue escalation process

## Level 3 — Integrated and Governed

Typical signs:

- AI use is embedded across teams
- formal approval exists
- procurement and data protection are involved
- AI risks are part of organisational governance
- trustees receive updates
- use cases are reviewed systematically

Main risks:

- complex integrations
- automation without enough oversight
- inconsistent supplier assurance
- bias or harm in operational workflows
- overreliance on AI

Priority actions:

1. Maintain a full AI system inventory.
2. Use DPIA triage for higher-risk use.
3. Integrate AI risk into governance.
4. Monitor outcomes and incidents.
5. Review suppliers and contracts.
6. Define meaningful human oversight.

Minimum evidence of progress:

- system inventory
- governance reporting
- specialist review
- monitoring
- formal escalation
- audit trail

## Level 4 — Assured and Learning

Typical signs:

- AI governance is mature
- controls are embedded
- risk is reviewed continuously
- independent assurance may be used
- research and feedback shape practice
- the organisation contributes to sector learning

Main risks:

- complacency
- over-complex governance
- outdated assumptions
- scaling risk
- weak transparency with affected people

Priority actions:

1. Commission periodic independent review.
2. Publish transparency information where appropriate.
3. Share learning with the sector.
4. Review strategic value and harm.
5. Test governance against new tools and regulation.
6. Improve controls using evidence and user feedback.

Minimum evidence of progress:

- assurance reports
- continuous monitoring
- transparent governance
- published learning
- clear accountability
- strategic review

# Maturity Dimensions

Assess maturity across several dimensions.

## Leadership and Ownership

Questions:

- Is someone responsible for AI?
- Do leaders understand current use?
- Are trustees informed?
- Is accountability clear?

## Policy and Governance

Questions:

- Is there an AI policy?
- Is it current?
- Are approval and escalation routes clear?
- Are higher-risk uses reviewed?

## Tools and Procurement

Questions:

- Are tools approved?
- Are plan differences understood?
- Are supplier terms reviewed?
- Are integrations controlled?

## Data Protection

Questions:

- Do staff recognise personal data?
- Is data minimisation used?
- Are DPIAs considered?
- Are high-risk uses escalated?

## Safeguarding

Questions:

- Are safeguarding risks explicit?
- Do staff know what not to paste?
- Are safeguarding leads involved?
- Are AI outputs kept out of critical decisions?

## Skills and Confidence

Questions:

- Are staff trained?
- Are volunteers included?
- Can people identify hallucinations and bias?
- Do staff know when to stop?

## Human Oversight

Questions:

- Who reviews outputs?
- Who owns decisions?
- Can staff challenge AI output?
- Are important decisions documented?

## Monitoring and Learning

Questions:

- Are incidents logged?
- Are use cases reviewed?
- Is feedback collected?
- Are lessons fed back into policy?

# Scoring Approach

Each maturity dimension can be scored from 0 to 4.

Example:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | Unmanaged |
| 1 | Aware |
| 2 | Controlled |
| 3 | Integrated |
| 4 | Assured |

Do not average scores blindly.

A charity may be mature in one area and weak in another.

High-risk weaknesses should be prioritised even if the overall score looks strong.

# Small Charity Path

A realistic first-year path may be:

## First 30 Days

- identify current tool use
- name an owner
- share do-not-paste rules
- stop obvious high-risk use

## First 90 Days

- approve a short policy
- approve a small tool list
- brief staff and volunteers
- log experiments

## First 6 Months

- review what is working
- add role-based guidance
- record incidents and questions
- update policy

## First 12 Months

- complete a maturity review
- report to trustees
- improve tools and training
- plan the next stage

# Medium-Sized Charity Path

Priorities may include:

- department-level owners
- standardised tool approval
- formal AI-use register
- role-based training
- procurement review
- trustee reporting
- quarterly assurance

# Large and Complex Charity Path

Priorities may include:

- full system inventory
- multidisciplinary governance
- formal DPIAs
- algorithmic impact assessment
- supplier assurance
- security architecture
- monitoring
- independent review
- transparency obligations

# Avoiding Maturity Theatre

Do not allow the model to become a badge or paperwork exercise.

A charity should not claim maturity based only on:

- having a policy
- buying an enterprise tool
- appointing an AI lead
- running one training session
- creating a register that is never used

Real maturity means:

- staff behaviour matches policy
- risks are understood
- decisions remain human
- guidance is current
- incidents lead to learning
- governance is proportionate and active

# Maturity Review Frequency

Recommended:

- Level 0–1: every 3 months
- Level 2: every 6 months
- Level 3–4: every 6 to 12 months, with continuous monitoring

Review sooner after:

- a serious incident
- a major provider change
- new high-risk use
- regulatory change
- significant organisational growth
- new sensitive services

# Monthly Claude Prompt

Use:

```text
Review the charity's current AI maturity using the Digital and AI Maturity Model.

Assess:

- leadership and ownership
- policy and governance
- tools and procurement
- data protection
- safeguarding
- skills and confidence
- human oversight
- monitoring and learning

Identify:

1. Current maturity by dimension.
2. Highest-risk weaknesses.
3. Practical next steps.
4. Actions suitable for the charity's size.
5. Evidence needed to show progress.
6. Items requiring trustee or specialist review.

Do not reward paperwork without real operational evidence.
Do not recommend enterprise controls as the default for small charities.
```

# Success Criteria

The maturity model succeeds when a charity can:

- identify its current position
- understand its biggest risks
- choose realistic next steps
- avoid unnecessary complexity
- scale governance over time
- show trustees what progress looks like
- revisit maturity as AI use changes

The model should make progress feel achievable without making safety optional.
