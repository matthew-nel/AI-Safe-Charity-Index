# Purpose

This document defines how charity-sector knowledge should shape the AI Safe Use Knowledge Base for UK Charities.

The resource must not treat AI, GDPR, safeguarding or governance as isolated subjects.

It should understand how charities actually work, including:

- governance
- trusteeship
- fundraising
- communications
- service delivery
- safeguarding
- volunteers
- finance
- HR
- digital strategy
- partnerships
- research
- data protection
- public trust

The purpose of this file is to make the knowledge base charity-first rather than technology-first.

# Core Principle

> Help charities make better decisions about AI in the context of how charities actually work.

Claude should not give generic business advice where charity-specific knowledge is available.

# Primary Domain Assumption

The primary audience includes small and medium-sized UK charities that may have:

- limited staff
- limited budgets
- high levels of responsibility
- sensitive service-user data
- volunteer involvement
- trustee oversight
- complex funding arrangements
- limited legal support
- limited digital capacity
- significant safeguarding duties
- a strong dependence on public trust

Recommendations must reflect these realities.

# Charity Size and Capacity

## Small Charities

Often have:

- few staff
- volunteer leadership
- informal systems
- limited budgets
- no specialist legal or data team
- reliance on free tools
- fragmented documentation

Guidance should prioritise:

- simple rules
- low-cost controls
- named responsibility
- approved tool lists
- basic training
- clear escalation

## Medium-Sized Charities

Often have:

- multiple departments
- larger data volumes
- several systems
- more formal governance
- more complex suppliers
- organisation-wide inconsistency

Guidance should prioritise:

- standardised policy
- role-based guidance
- risk registers
- tool approval
- cross-team oversight
- quarterly review

## Large and Complex Charities

May have:

- international operations
- large-scale data processing
- dedicated legal and security teams
- custom systems
- formal procurement
- significant automated decision-making
- multiple regulators
- complex data-sharing agreements

Guidance should include advanced routes without allowing enterprise complexity to dominate the main experience.

# Charity-Specific Editorial Rules

When writing guidance:

- use charity examples before commercial examples
- explain how trustees, staff and volunteers are affected
- consider safeguarding
- consider donor and beneficiary trust
- consider limited capacity
- consider proportionate governance
- avoid assuming enterprise tools
- avoid generic corporate language
- show realistic low-cost first steps
- separate small-charity minimums from advanced practice

# Knowledge Ingestion

When new charity-sector material is uploaded, Claude should:

1. Identify the domain.
2. Identify the audience.
3. Record the source.
4. Summarise the key operational insight.
5. Separate sector practice from legal requirement.
6. Identify relevance to AI use.
7. Identify affected pages or templates.
8. Record limitations.
9. Add long-term insights to the knowledge map.
10. Avoid overgeneralising from one charity or one source.

# Limits

This file does not replace:

- current regulator guidance
- specialist legal advice
- safeguarding procedures
- sector-specific regulation
- organisation-specific policies
- professional judgement

Charity knowledge should provide context, not create unsupported certainty.

# Success Criteria

This document succeeds when Claude:

- understands how charities operate
- writes charity-specific guidance
- avoids generic commercial examples
- considers trustees, volunteers and service users
- scales advice by organisation size
- connects AI guidance to real workflows
- recognises safeguarding and trust as core concerns
- uses new charity-sector knowledge to improve future content

The knowledge base should feel as though it was built from inside the charity sector, not adapted from generic business guidance.
