# UK Charity AI Safety Landscape and Tool Implementation Report – July 2026

## 1. Overview and Scope

This report synthesises the core UK charity AI-safety landscape as at July 2026, combining regulatory updates, sector trends, implementation recommendations, and high-level reviews of major AI tools commonly used by UK charities (ChatGPT/OpenAI, Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini/Workspace, Microsoft Copilot for Microsoft 365).[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8]
It is intended to support the AI Safe Charity Index knowledge base, providing reusable Markdown analysis aligned with the "Before You Paste" framework, GDPR decision rules and safeguarding guidance, while clearly flagging areas requiring specialist legal, GDPR or safeguarding review.[^6][^9][^10][^11]

## 2. Current AI Adoption in UK Charities (July 2026)

Charity-sector surveys and commentary indicate rapid growth in AI adoption over the last three years, with mainstream use now common across charities of all sizes.[^12][^2][^13][^1]

- Interim analysis for the Charity Digital Skills Report 2026 suggests that 79% of voluntary organisations routinely use AI, up from 76% in the previous year and 61% in 2024.[^2][^12]
- Commentary from Zoe Amar and Third Sector AI reports that around 88% of charities are using AI tools daily, with adoption particularly high for administration, content creation and project management tasks.[^13][^1]
- However, only a minority of charities appear to use AI strategically or with robust governance; one article notes that only about 3% of trustees report their charity using AI at all, signalling a gap between staff-level usage and board awareness.[^2][^13]
- Surveys show growing concern about accuracy, bias and environmental impact, and that GDPR and data-privacy worries are now among the biggest barriers to deeper AI adoption and experimentation in charities.[^12][^13][^2]

These findings support an implementation stance that assumes widespread, often informal AI use inside charities, uneven governance and board awareness, and significant anxiety about getting GDPR and safeguarding decisions wrong.

## 3. Regulatory and Policy Landscape (UK, July 2026)

### 3.1 ICO: AI and Data Protection Guidance

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) has consolidated and expanded its guidance on AI and data protection, providing a detailed framework for organisations processing personal data using AI systems.[^14][^15][^6]
Key elements include:

- A lifecycle approach to AI, emphasising data protection by design and by default across exploration, development, deployment, monitoring and retirement.[^6]
- Requirements for lawful basis, transparency, fairness, accuracy, and accountability for AI-assisted and automated decision-making involving personal data, with explicit links to Article 22 and profiling provisions.[^16][^6]
- Strong expectations around DPIAs for high-risk AI uses and guidance on explaining AI decisions to individuals, particularly where decisions significantly affect them.[^16][^6]

ICO has also published a strategic approach to regulating AI, highlighting pragmatic, risk-based enforcement and a focus on high-harm AI applications.  Recent commentary summarising ICO's interim views on generative AI and personal data underscores the need for clear lawful basis, fairness and transparency when training or deploying generative models with personal data.[^17][^18][^19]

### 3.2 Wider UK AI Regulation and Government Policy

Beyond ICO, wider UK AI regulation and government policy has continued to evolve in 2025–2026:

- Regulatory outlook updates note evolving UK approaches to automated decision-making and AI-related copyright, alongside emerging expectations from competition and digital regulators about AI agents and transparency.[^20][^21][^22]
- Government speech in April 2026 outlines ambitions for the UK to set standards for AI deployment, with the AI Security Institute and international AI security networks publishing best practice for model evaluation and safety testing.[^23]

Although not charity-specific, these developments indicate a growing expectation that organisations deploying AI—including charities—will align with evolving standards for model safety testing, transparency and governance as part of responsible use.

### 3.3 Charity Commission: AI and Governance

The Charity Commission’s blog "Charities and Artificial Intelligence" provides the most explicit charity-regulator guidance currently available.[^7]
It emphasises that:

- Trustees must treat AI as part of their overall governance and risk-management responsibilities, ensuring use of AI furthers the charity’s purposes and does not undermine beneficiary trust.[^7]
- AI-generated outputs, including advice, should never be accepted uncritically, and trustees should maintain human oversight and judgement.[^7]
- Safeguarding, data protection and reputational risks must be considered before adopting AI, especially where activities involve vulnerable people or sensitive issues.[^7]

This positions AI as a governance issue rather than purely a technical or digital matter, implying that trustee boards need AI policies, risk frameworks and visibility over AI use across the organisation.

### 3.4 Fundraising Regulator: AI in Fundraising

The Fundraising Regulator has issued dedicated guidance on using AI in fundraising, along with an AI policy and supporting communications.[^8][^24][^25]
Key expectations include:

- Charities remain responsible for AI-generated fundraising content and must ensure it complies with the Code of Fundraising Practice (e.g. accuracy, fairness, non-misleading communications).[^26][^8]
- AI should be used within a lifecycle framework: exploring, preparing, implementing, monitoring and reviewing AI-enabled fundraising, with trustee oversight and risk assessments at each stage.[^25][^8]
- Transparency to donors about AI involvement and appropriate training for staff using AI tools in fundraising are highlighted as good practice.[^8][^25]

Civil Society reporting suggests regulators are encouraging charities to publish AI fundraising policies and address "bad actor" fears, reinforcing the need for governance and clear rules around AI use in fundraising.[^27]

### 3.5 Sector Bodies and Professional Commentary

Sector organisations such as NCVO, Charity Digital Skills and Charity Excellence provide context about AI adoption and challenges in UK charities.[^28][^29][^30][^12]
Themes include:

- Rapid uptake of AI tools but significant gaps in AI skills, strategies and governance, especially among smaller charities.[^12][^2]
- Digital and AI gaps between large and small charities, although 2026 data suggests adoption levels may be converging even as governance concerns persist.[^1][^12]
- Calls for ethical frameworks, training and practical guidance to balance innovation with protection of vulnerable people and data privacy.[^31][^32][^33]

Professional legal and policy commentary continues to highlight challenges around generative AI and data protection, automated decision-making standards, deepfake risks and the tension between innovation and fundamental rights.[^34][^21][^35][^36][^20]

## 4. July 2026 Updates Relevant to Charities

Several developments in early–mid 2026 have direct or indirect implications for charity AI safety:

- Charity Digital Skills Report interim 2026 data shows AI adoption continuing to rise (around 79% of organisations using AI routinely), but trust in AI tools has dropped, with 35% reporting a lack of trust in AI compared to 15% the previous year.[^2]
- The same report indicates that many charities lack digital strategies and AI governance frameworks; around a third of boards and a quarter of CEOs have poor AI skills, and a significant proportion of charities have no trustee with digital skills.[^2]
- Sector commentary notes that data privacy and GDPR concerns are now one of the largest barriers to further AI adoption, with nearly half of charities reporting that GDPR fears prevent them from using AI more widely.[^13]
- Research on AI-powered personalisation in charity marketing highlights both the benefits of AI-driven donor engagement and the need for regulators to promote ethical guidelines and transparency in donor-focused AI adoption.[^31]
- Charity Excellence’s "Future Charity" report (Part 1, April 2026) discusses the move from "quiet use" of AI to strategic leadership, suggesting that many charities use AI informally but have not yet embedded it into leadership-level planning and risk processes.[^28]
- Government and legal updates on AI regulation in 2026 (including AI Security Institute work and AI-related provisions in new acts) signal an environment where AI safety, model evaluation and deepfake controls are likely to become more prominent expectations for all organisations.[^21][^20][^23]

These changes underline that the charity sector is now in an "embedded but anxious" phase of AI adoption, with widespread everyday use but significant governance, skills and trust gaps.

## 5. Risk Themes for UK Charities Using AI

Based on regulator guidance, sector surveys and professional analysis, key risk themes include:

- **Data protection and privacy risk:** Unclear lawful basis for AI-related processing, over-collection in prompts, use of personal data in training, and complex international transfer routes in cloud-based AI tools.[^19][^6][^16]
- **Safeguarding risk:** Use of AI in services involving vulnerable people or sensitive topics (e.g. relationships, health, mental health) without appropriate safeguarding frameworks, potentially leading to harmful or inappropriate content or misclassification of risk.[^37][^38][^7]
- **Governance risk:** Trustees not aware of AI use, lack of AI policies and registers, insufficient board skills, and weak oversight over AI-enabled fundraising and service delivery.[^13][^2][^7]
- **Accuracy and quality risk:** Hallucinations, outdated or jurisdictionally incorrect content, over-reliance on AI outputs for advice or policy drafting, and failure to review content before publication or service use.[^39][^40][^7]
- **Bias and discrimination risk:** Training data and models that embed historical biases, potentially leading to discriminatory donor profiling, targeting or service eligibility decisions.[^35][^33][^31]
- **Procurement and tool-choice risk:** Use of free or consumer accounts for work data, misunderstanding of plan-level privacy differences, and relying on shadow AI tools without contracts or DPAs.[^41][^42][^19][^13]
- **Reputational and trust risk:** Public incidents involving AI misuse, perceived dehumanisation of services, and donor concerns about AI in fundraising and communications.[^27][^2][^7]

## 6. High-Level Tool Landscape (Privacy and Data Protection)

This section summarises headline privacy and data-protection features for major AI tools used by charities, based on official enterprise/business documentation and recent updates. Detailed tool review packs should be produced separately before publication of any public guidance.

### 6.1 OpenAI – ChatGPT, Business and Enterprise

OpenAI’s 2026 "Enterprise privacy" statement covers ChatGPT Business, ChatGPT Enterprise, ChatGPT for Healthcare, ChatGPT Edu, ChatGPT for Teachers and API platform usage.[^3]
Key commitments for these business/enterprise offerings include:

- Inputs and outputs for covered business offerings are not used to train OpenAI’s models by default; customers own and control their business data.[^3]
- Customers can configure data retention periods and control which internal sources are connected, with SAML SSO and fine-grained access controls for enterprise authentication.[^3]
- Business data is encrypted at rest and in transit, and OpenAI highlights SOC 2 compliance and other security measures via its Trust Portal.[^3]

Charity implications:

- For GDPR-sensitive work, charities should prefer Business/Enterprise/API offerings with clear data-processing terms rather than using consumer ChatGPT accounts for operational data. Specialist review is needed to confirm controller/processor roles and specific DPAs.[^3]

### 6.2 Anthropic – Claude

Anthropic’s July 8, 2026 consumer privacy policy update distinguishes between consumer plans (Claude Free, Pro, Max) and business plans (Claude Team, Enterprise, Developer Platform), with different data treatment and contractual terms.[^42][^41]
Key points drawn from Anthropic’s own summary and secondary analyses include:

- Consumer accounts may allow conversation and agentic task data to be used to improve models if users opt in, with a five-year retention for such data.[^41]
- The policy adds explicit clauses around data flows when Claude executes multi-step tasks via connected third-party apps and around age/identity verification data.[^42][^41]
- Business/enterprise plans are governed by separate commercial terms and are not covered by the consumer privacy policy update.[^43][^41]

Sector commentary stresses that many organisations’ staff use consumer Free/Pro accounts for work without central tracking, creating hidden GDPR and confidentiality risks.[^44][^42][^13]

Charity implications:

- Charities should treat consumer Claude accounts as high-risk for organisational data and require staff to use team or enterprise plans with appropriate DPAs and governance, especially when connecting email, calendars or storage. Specialist privacy review is needed to assess law enforcement/cooperation clauses and agentic data flows.[^44][^41][^42]

### 6.3 Google – Gemini in Workspace

Google Workspace with Gemini emphasises enterprise-ready privacy, security and compliance for generative AI use within Workspace.[^5][^45]
Key points include:

- Workspace data (emails, documents, etc.) is not used to train or improve generative AI models outside Workspace without permission; Gemini in Gmail, Docs, etc. uses content to respond to prompts but does not use that content to train models.[^45][^5]
- Workspace emphasises strong data security, including granular DLP, access controls, client-side encryption options, AI classification and data boundary options (EU/US) for sovereign data control.[^5]
- Gemini activity is logged with export capabilities so administrators can monitor AI usage and apply security controls.[^5]

Charity implications:

- For charities already using Google Workspace, Gemini can be deployed with existing identity, DLP and audit capabilities, provided administrators carefully configure access, boundaries and experiments programmes. Local policies should differentiate personal-account Gemini apps from Workspace Gemini and clarify when data may be used for training.[^45][^5]

### 6.4 Microsoft – Copilot for Microsoft 365

Microsoft’s enterprise documentation describes "enterprise data protection" (EDP) for Microsoft 365 Copilot and Copilot Chat, with prompts and responses treated similarly to other Microsoft 365 data under standard DPA and Product Terms.[^4][^46][^47][^48]
Key points include:

- Prompts and responses for work/school accounts are protected under enterprise terms, with encryption at rest and in transit and data isolation between tenants.[^48][^4]
- Prompts, responses and Graph data accessed through Copilot are not used to train foundation models; Copilot respects existing permissions, labels, retention policies and administrative settings.[^47][^4]
- Web search queries to Bing used as part of Copilot responses have separate data-handling practices, with Microsoft acting as independent data controller; these queries are de-identified and not used to train foundation models.[^4][^48]

Charity implications:

- Charities already on Microsoft 365 can leverage Copilot within the same governance framework as existing email and file systems, but should update internal AI policies to address web queries, agent behaviours and logging/audit capabilities. DPAs and Product Terms should be reviewed for GDPR compliance and data boundary commitments.[^46][^48][^4]

## 7. Implementation Recommendations – Governance and Policies

### 7.1 Board and Trustee-Level Actions

- Ensure AI use is explicitly included in risk registers, governance frameworks and trustee training, with clear ownership for AI governance (e.g. digital lead, CIO, DPO, safeguarding lead).[^49][^2][^7]
- Require an AI policy approved by the board, covering permitted tools, prohibited uses (e.g. pasting safeguarding disclosures), DPIA triggers, and escalation routes for complex cases.[^12][^13][^2]
- Maintain an AI register documenting tools used, plan/account types, data flows, DPAs/terms and risk ratings (green/amber/red), aligned with ICO and Fundraising Regulator expectations.[^25][^6][^8]

### 7.2 Data Protection and DPIA Practice

- Treat AI projects involving personal data as likely DPIA candidates, especially for profiling, automated decisions, work with vulnerable people, children or sensitive categories.[^19][^6][^16]
- Adopt a lifecycle DPIA template for AI, covering exploration, procurement, deployment, monitoring and sunsetting, with explicit sections on training data, prompts, retention, international transfers and user rights.[^17][^6][^8]
- Require plan-by-plan comparisons for tools (consumer vs business vs enterprise) to ensure organisational data is processed under appropriate contractual terms and with training opt-outs where needed.[^41][^4][^5][^3]

### 7.3 Safeguarding and Vulnerable People

- Mandate safeguarding review for any AI use in services involving vulnerable people, children or sensitive disclosures (e.g. relationship support, mental health, safeguarding referrals).[^38][^37][^7]
- Prohibit feeding safeguarding case data or identifiable disclosures into general-purpose AI tools unless part of a heavily controlled, specialist project with DPIA and safeguarding oversight, and likely requiring enterprise-level agreements.[^38][^6][^19]
- Include AI-related scenarios in safeguarding training, focusing on deepfake risks, misclassification, and incident reporting if AI outputs cause harm or confusion.[^37][^38][^7]

### 7.4 Everyday Practice and "Before You Paste"

- Reinforce "Before You Paste" rules that prohibit staff from pasting:
  - Identifiable beneficiary data.
  - Safeguarding disclosures.
  - Highly sensitive donor, staff or health data.
  - Confidential board or strategy documents.
Into consumer or unapproved AI tools.[^6][^38][^13][^7]
- Provide practical examples of safe prompts (green) focusing on internal brainstorming and public-information summarisation, and amber prompts that require review and DPIA (e.g. segmenting donors or drafting sensitive service communications).[^30][^31][^8]
- Emphasise that staff must never treat AI-generated content as authoritative advice, especially for law, safeguarding, clinical or employment questions; human review is mandatory.[^40][^39][^7]

## 8. Tool-Specific Recommendations for Charities

### 8.1 General Preference for Enterprise/Business Plans

Across major tools, enterprise or business plans provide stronger privacy, data protection and admin controls than consumer offerings:

- OpenAI: Use ChatGPT Business/Enterprise/API with explicit commitments not to train models on business data by default and with configurable retention.[^3]
- Anthropic: Use Claude Team/Enterprise or Developer Platform for organisational work; avoid consumer Free/Pro/Max accounts for charity data due to training options, agentic data flows and consumer-oriented law enforcement clauses.[^42][^44][^41]
- Google: Use Gemini in Workspace with AI plans, benefiting from Workspace’s existing identity, DLP and logging; avoid personal-account Gemini apps for organisational data unless policies explicitly permit and DPIAs have been completed.[^45][^5]
- Microsoft: Use Microsoft 365 Copilot and Copilot Chat with enterprise data protection tied to Microsoft 365 DPA and Product Terms; avoid standalone consumer Copilot for work data.[^46][^48][^4]

### 8.2 Green / Amber / Red Patterns by Tool

- **Green:**
  - Internal drafting and summarisation of non-sensitive documents using enterprise tools configured with strong access controls and audit logs.[^4][^5][^3]
  - Generative support for meeting notes, agenda drafting and plain-English rewrites where data is already internal and governance is in place.[^1][^4][^5]

- **Amber:**
  - Donor communications, fundraising copy and grant applications drafted with AI, subject to human review, Code compliance and transparent donor messaging.[^26][^31][^8]
  - Internal analytics on pseudonymised data via AI tooling, with clear assessment of re-identification risk and DPIA coverage.[^35][^6]

- **Red:**
  - Use of consumer AI accounts for charity emails, calendars or storage without organisational control or DPAs.[^13][^41][^42]
  - Training or fine-tuning models on raw case notes, counselling transcripts or safeguarding reports without robust DPIA, safeguarding and legal review and without enterprise-level contractual protections.[^35][^19][^38]

## 9. Items Requiring Specialist Legal / GDPR / Safeguarding Review

The following areas should be flagged for specialist review before any public guidance is finalised:

- Lawful basis and fairness for using donor and beneficiary data in training or fine-tuning generative models, particularly under legitimate interests vs consent.[^19][^6]
- Interpretation of Anthropic’s consumer privacy policy law enforcement and third-party sharing clauses, including risk of disclosure without court orders and distinction from enterprise terms.[^43][^44][^41][^42]
- Implications of UK Data (Use and Access) Act provisions (where applicable) and evolving rules on automated decision-making, deepfakes and AI transparency for charity use-cases.[^50][^20][^21]
- Cross-border data transfers for AI services (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft), particularly for sensitive categories and vulnerable groups, and interactions with EU/UK data boundaries.[^4][^5][^3]
- Safeguarding implications of AI-mediated support services (e.g. chatbots for youth or relationship services), including escalation procedures, incident management and appropriateness of automated triage.[^51][^37][^38][^7]

## 10. Suggested Codex Implementation Focus

For the AI Safe Charity Index, this research suggests Codex should prioritise:

- Updating AI-and-data-protection pages to reflect ICO’s lifecycle AI guidance, generative AI interim views and sector adoption data.[^16][^6][^19][^2]
- Strengthening "Before You Paste" guidance with explicit red lines on safeguarding and personal data, examples of green/amber/red prompts, and references to regulator expectations.[^8][^6][^7]
- Creating new governance pages for AI strategy, AI DPIAs and AI safeguarding, targeted at trustees, DPOs and safeguarding leads.[^49][^38][^7]
- Adding tool-specific pages for OpenAI, Anthropic, Google Workspace/Gemini and Microsoft 365 Copilot, focusing on plan-level data protection, training policies and admin controls.[^48][^41][^5][^4][^3]
- Integrating charity-sector adoption statistics and trust concerns into learning paths, FAQs and trustee briefings to ground guidance in current practice and anxieties.[^1][^12][^2][^13]

These implementation steps should remain subject to human review and sign-off, especially where they touch on legal interpretations, GDPR decision rules or safeguarding guidance.
