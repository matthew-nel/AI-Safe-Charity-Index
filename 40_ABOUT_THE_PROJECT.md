# About the Project

The AI Safe Use Knowledge Base for UK Charities is a practical, evidence-led resource that helps charities use artificial intelligence safely, confidently and responsibly.

It is built primarily for small and medium-sized UK charities, while also providing deeper guidance for larger and more complex organisations.

The project translates complex information about AI, data protection, safeguarding, governance and digital practice into guidance that charity teams can understand and use.

---

# Mission

> **Help charities use AI safely, confidently and responsibly through practical, evidence-led guidance they can trust.**

# Vision

> **Make trusted AI guidance simple enough for every charity to use with confidence.**

# What the Project Does Not Cover

The project is not:

- legal advice
- a replacement for safeguarding procedures
- a replacement for a Data Protection Impact Assessment
- a replacement for professional HR advice
- a substitute for security assurance
- a product certification scheme
- a guarantee that a tool is safe
- a general AI training course
- a prompt engineering library
- a marketing platform for AI vendors

For complex or high-risk use, charities should seek appropriate specialist advice.

# Why the Project Is Needed

AI adoption is moving faster than many charities can realistically manage.

Large organisations may have:

- legal teams
- information security teams
- procurement specialists
- data-protection officers
- enterprise systems
- formal governance

Many charities do not.

Small and medium-sized organisations often need to make practical decisions with limited time, limited budgets and limited specialist support.

The project exists to make those decisions easier without pretending that complex risks have simple answers.

# Charity-First Approach

The project begins with charity work rather than AI technology.

Guidance is organised around real tasks such as:

- writing a funding bid
- creating a campaign
- analysing survey responses
- using meeting transcription
- preparing board papers
- drafting policies
- creating training
- managing volunteers
- supporting service delivery

The question is not only:

> What can this AI tool do?

The more useful question is:

> Is this a sensible, safe and proportionate way for this charity to complete this task?

# Small and Medium-Sized Charities First

The project is designed primarily for organisations that may have:

- few staff
- volunteer leadership
- limited budgets
- informal systems

- no full-time data-protection specialist

- formal procurement
- enterprise controls
- technical assurance
- DPIAs
- audit
- system inventories
- independent review

# The Before You Paste Framework

The signature feature of the project is the Before You Paste framework.

Before entering information into an AI tool, ask:

- Is personal data involved?
- Is special category data involved?
- Is safeguarding information involved?
- Is the information confidential?
- Is the tool approved?
- Can less information be used?
- Can the information be anonymised?
- Could the output affect a person?
- Is meaningful human review in place?
- Would you be comfortable explaining this use?

The framework uses:

- **Green — Go ahead with normal checks**
- **Amber — Pause and check**
- **Red — Stop**

The classification applies to the use case, not to the brand name of the tool.

# Editorial Approach

The writing should feel like:

> A calm, experienced digital colleague helping a charity make a sensible decision.

The style should be:

- practical
- calm
- direct
- warm
- evidence-led
- conversational
- proportionate
- transparent about uncertainty

The project avoids:

- AI hype
- legalistic writing where plain English will do
- generic corporate language
- fear-based messaging
- unsupported certainty
- overly technical explanations
- treating policy as the only solution

---

# Plain English

Complex terms should be explained when first used.

The project should:

- put the practical answer first
- use short sentences
- use one main idea per paragraph
- explain acronyms
- use charity-specific examples
- separate law from recommendation
- show what to do next

Plain English does not mean removing necessary accuracy.

# Feedback and Corrections

Users should be able to:

- report an error
- flag outdated guidance
- suggest a source
- ask a question
- suggest a new topic
- share a workflow
- join research
- propose a contribution

Corrections should be reviewed and recorded through the release process.

Important factual, safety, safeguarding or data-protection errors should not wait for the next normal monthly release.

# Versioning

The project uses semantic-style versioning:

```text
MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH
```

Examples:

- `1.0.0` — first public release
- `1.1.0` — new guidance or template
- `1.1.1` — correction or clarification
- `2.0.0` — major structural or framework change

Substantive pages also have their own versions and review dates.

# Human Oversight

AI can support:

- drafting
- summarising
- comparing
- structuring
- analysing
- documenting

AI should not replace:

- safeguarding decisions
- legal advice
- trustee accountability
- professional judgement
- employment decisions

# Accessibility

The public knowledge base should aim to meet WCAG 2.2 AA where practical.

Accessibility includes:

- semantic structure
- keyboard navigation
- readable colour contrast
- descriptive links
- alt text
- accessible forms
- no colour-only meaning
- plain English
- readable layouts
- mobile support

Accessibility feedback should be treated as a core quality issue.

- create proportionate governance

- contribute feedback
