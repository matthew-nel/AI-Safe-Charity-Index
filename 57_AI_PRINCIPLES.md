# Purpose

This document defines the core principles that should guide AI use across UK charities.

These principles should inform:

- staff guidance
- trustee oversight
- AI policies
- tool approval
- procurement
- service design
- research
- safeguarding
- data protection
- content creation
- automation
- public communication

The principles are designed to be practical enough for small charities and robust enough to scale to larger organisations.

---

# Core Principle

> **Use AI to support people, not to avoid responsibility.**

AI can help charities save time, improve access and increase capacity.

It should not weaken:

- human judgement
- accountability
- safeguarding
- privacy
- fairness
- public trust

---

# Principle 1 — Put People Before Technology

Start with the needs, rights and safety of people.

Do not begin with:

- what the tool can do
- what competitors are doing
- what is fashionable
- what appears efficient

Begin with:

- who could benefit
- who could be harmed
- whose data is involved
- what support people need
- what happens if the output is wrong

A technically possible use is not automatically an appropriate use.

---

# Principle 2 — Protect People Before Prompts

Before entering information into an AI tool, check:

- personal data
- special category data
- safeguarding information
- confidential information
- legal privilege
- credentials
- security-sensitive information

Use:

- public information
- fictional examples
- genuinely anonymised data
- aggregated information
- the minimum data needed

When in doubt, leave it out and ask for help.

---

# Principle 3 — Keep Humans Accountable

A person should remain responsible for:

- the task
- the data used
- the output
- the final decision
- the consequences

Do not delegate final responsibility to AI.

This is especially important for:

- safeguarding
- recruitment
- employment
- complaints
- eligibility
- funding
- risk assessment
- legal matters
- trustee decisions
- services affecting vulnerable people

---

# Principle 4 — Use the Least Data Necessary

Do not use more information than the task requires.

Ask:

- Can the task be completed with public information?
- Can details be removed?
- Can a fictional example be used?
- Can the information be aggregated?
- Can a smaller extract be used?
- Is AI needed at all?

Data minimisation reduces:

- privacy risk
- security risk
- accidental disclosure
- unnecessary dependence on providers

---

# Principle 5 — Treat Safeguarding as a Separate Duty

Safeguarding is not only a data-protection issue.

A use may be lawful under data-protection rules and still be unsafe from a safeguarding perspective.

AI must not:

- assess immediate safeguarding risk
- decide whether abuse occurred
- replace safeguarding leads
- replace statutory procedures
- process real case details casually
- provide case-specific emergency advice

Protecting people from harm takes priority.

---

# Principle 6 — Use Proportionate Governance

Governance should match:

- the level of risk
- the sensitivity of the data
- the importance of the decision
- the size of the organisation
- the complexity of the tool
- the likely impact on people

A small charity may need:

- one named owner
- a short policy
- a small approved-tools list
- clear do-not-paste rules
- simple staff training

A larger charity may need:

- procurement review
- DPIAs
- system inventory
- audit
- enterprise controls
- specialist assurance

Good governance should be achievable, not performative.

---

# Principle 7 — Be Clear About What AI Can and Cannot Do

Do not describe AI as:

- objective
- unbiased
- human-like
- always accurate
- automatically safe
- automatically compliant
- a replacement for expertise

Explain limitations clearly.

AI may:

- invent information
- omit context
- reproduce bias
- misunderstand instructions
- provide outdated answers
- sound more confident than the evidence allows

---

# Principle 8 — Review Outputs Meaningfully

Human review should be more than a quick glance.

Review for:

- factual accuracy
- source support
- bias
- tone
- accessibility
- confidentiality
- safeguarding
- legal meaning
- organisational context
- possible harm

The more important the use, the stronger the review should be.

---

# Principle 9 — Separate Fact, Interpretation and Recommendation

Public guidance should distinguish:

- what the law requires
- what regulators advise
- what providers state
- what sector evidence suggests
- what the project recommends

Do not present:

- provider marketing as evidence
- recommendations as law
- limited research as certainty
- interpretation as official guidance

Where uncertainty exists, explain it.

---

# Principle 10 — Choose Tools for the Task, Not the Brand

Do not treat a tool as simply safe or unsafe.

Assess:

- product
- plan
- account type
- data handling
- retention
- training-data statements
- admin controls
- permissions
- integrations
- the task
- the data
- the people affected

The same tool may be:

- Green for one task
- Amber for another
- Red for another

---

# Principle 11 — Use Approved Accounts and Controls

Where organisational information is involved, use:

- approved accounts
- approved tools
- appropriate permissions
- appropriate licences
- organisational administration
- clear access rules

Do not assume a personal free account is suitable for charity data.

Approval does not remove the need for judgement.

---

# Principle 12 — Be Transparent

Where AI use materially affects people, explain:

- that AI is involved
- what it is doing
- what data is used
- what the limitations are
- who remains responsible
- how a person can ask questions or challenge a decision

Transparency should be proportionate and understandable.

---

# Principle 13 — Preserve Meaningful Choice and Challenge

People should be able to:

- ask for an explanation
- question an output
- request human review
- correct inaccurate information
- challenge significant decisions

Do not design systems where AI output becomes difficult to contest.

---

# Principle 14 — Test for Fairness

Ask:

- Could this disadvantage a group?
- Is the source data biased?
- Are examples representative?
- Are accessibility needs considered?
- Are assumptions being made about people?
- Could the output reinforce stereotypes?
- Does the tool work equally well for different users?

Fairness is not guaranteed by using the same process for everyone.

---

# Principle 15 — Design for Accessibility

AI-supported content and services should remain accessible.

Review:

- plain English
- headings
- links
- alt text
- captions
- keyboard access
- screen-reader use
- cognitive load
- mobile use
- colour contrast

Efficiency should not create new barriers.

---

# Principle 16 — Keep Security in Scope

Do not paste:

- passwords
- access tokens
- API keys
- private keys
- credentials
- confidential security details
- production secrets

Review:

- access controls
- integrations
- connected systems
- document permissions
- account ownership
- offboarding
- incident response

AI safety is also a security issue.

---

# Principle 17 — Record Important Use

When AI use is regular, important or higher risk, record:

- tool
- task
- data
- owner
- approval
- risk level
- review date
- outcome
- incidents
- lessons learned

An AI-use register supports accountability and learning.

---

# Principle 18 — Learn From Incidents and Near Misses

When something goes wrong:

- protect people first
- contain the issue
- report it
- investigate
- correct public guidance
- update policy
- improve training
- record the lesson

Do not treat mistakes as reasons to hide AI use.

Transparency helps prevent repetition.

---

# Principle 19 — Review Tools and Guidance Regularly

AI products change quickly.

Review:

- provider terms
- privacy documentation
- retention
- admin controls
- product features
- regulator guidance
- charity-sector practice
- current user feedback

A tool review without a current date should not be treated as permanent guidance.

---

# Principle 20 — Avoid Automation for Its Own Sake

Do not automate a process only because it can be automated.

Ask:

- Is the process worth improving?
- Is the existing process fair?
- Does automation remove useful human contact?
- Could errors scale?
- Does the charity have capacity to monitor it?
- Is the benefit real?

Automating a poor process can make it worse faster.

---

# Principle 21 — Respect Copyright and Creative Work

Check:

- rights in source material
- rights in uploaded documents
- permission to reuse content
- attribution
- provider terms
- image and media rights
- licence conditions

Do not upload paid books, restricted reports or copyrighted materials without permission.

Do not assume AI-generated content is automatically free of copyright risk.

---

# Principle 22 — Keep Research Ethical

When using AI in research:

- collect only necessary data
- obtain appropriate consent
- protect confidentiality
- avoid exposing raw responses
- document method
- explain limitations
- verify AI-generated analysis
- do not fabricate findings
- do not overstate small samples

AI can support research, but it does not replace research ethics.

---

# Principle 23 — Protect Editorial Independence

Tool recommendations and safety ratings should not be influenced by:

- affiliate income
- sponsorship
- vendor pressure
- consultancy relationships
- advertising

Commercial relationships should be disclosed.

Evidence standards should remain the same for every provider.

---

# Principle 24 — Make Corrections Visible

If guidance is wrong:

- correct it
- version it
- update the changelog
- explain material changes
- notify users where action is required
- improve the process that failed

Do not silently replace important guidance.

---

# Principle 25 — Start Small and Learn

Begin with:

- low-risk tasks
- public information
- a small number of tools
- clear review
- clear ownership
- short experiments
- documented learning

Scale only when:

- the benefit is clear
- controls work
- staff understand the use
- risks are manageable
- governance is proportionate

---

# Practical Decision Test

Before approving an AI use, ask:

1. What problem are we solving?
2. Is AI necessary?
3. What data is involved?
4. Could someone be identified?
5. Is safeguarding involved?
6. Is the tool approved?
7. What could go wrong?
8. Could the output affect a person?
9. Who reviews it?
10. Who is accountable?
11. Can we explain the use?
12. How will we review it later?

---

# Green, Amber and Red Application

## Green

Use where:

- information is public or fictional
- the task is low risk
- human review is straightforward
- errors are unlikely to cause harm

## Amber

Use where:

- information is internal
- context matters
- anonymisation is required
- an approved tool is needed
- manager or specialist review may be required

## Red

Stop where:

- safeguarding information is involved
- highly sensitive personal data is involved
- credentials are involved
- legal privilege is involved
- AI would make a significant decision
- an error could cause serious harm
- governance is absent

# Trustee Application

Trustees should use these principles to ask:

- Are we using AI for a clear purpose?
- Are risks proportionate?
- Are staff trained?
- Are tools approved?
- Is personal data protected?
- Is safeguarding protected?
- Are incidents reported?
- Are high-risk uses reviewed?
- Are we transparent?
- Are we learning?

---

# Staff Application

Staff should use these principles to decide:

- what they can paste
- which tool to use
- when to stop
- who to ask
- how to review outputs
- how to report a problem

---

# Procurement Application

Before buying or approving a tool, check:

- data handling
- retention
- security
- accessibility
- admin controls
- account type
- integrations
- contracts
- supplier transparency
- exit arrangements
- suitability for the actual task

---

# Public Communication Application

When AI helps create public content:

- verify facts
- review tone
- disclose AI use where appropriate
- protect people's stories
- check representation
- check accessibility
- keep accountability human

---

# Principle Conflicts

Principles may sometimes compete.

Examples:

- speed versus review
- personalisation versus data minimisation
- transparency versus confidentiality
- innovation versus safeguarding
- automation versus human contact

When principles conflict:

1. protect people from harm
2. follow law and regulation
3. preserve accountability
4. minimise data
5. document the decision
6. seek specialist review where needed

---

# Review and Versioning

Review these principles:

- quarterly
- after major regulator changes
- after serious incidents
- after significant research
- before major releases
- when governance changes

Material changes should be:

- versioned
- recorded in the changelog
- reflected in policy templates
- reflected in tool reviews
- reflected in staff guidance

# Success Criteria

These principles succeed when:

- staff can make safer everyday decisions
- trustees can govern without becoming technical experts
- data protection and safeguarding remain central
- human responsibility remains visible
- tools are assessed proportionately
- innovation does not outrun accountability
- corrections and learning improve the project
- charities can use AI with confidence without losing trust
