# Purpose

The **Before You Paste** framework is the core practical decision system for the knowledge base.

It helps charity staff, volunteers, managers and trustees decide whether information can be safely entered into a general-purpose AI tool.

The framework is designed primarily for small and medium-sized charities, but it can also be used as a first-line check by larger organisations before deeper legal, security, procurement or data-protection review.

The framework does not replace professional advice, a Data Protection Impact Assessment, safeguarding procedures, supplier due diligence or formal organisational approval.

# Core Principle

> Before you paste information into an AI tool, stop and check what the information contains, where it may go, what the tool may do with it, and what could happen if the output is wrong.

The framework should help users answer:

- What am I trying to do?
- What information am I using?
- Could someone be identified?
- Is the information sensitive or confidential?
- Is the tool approved?
- Could the output affect a person?
- Who needs to review the result?
- Should I stop and ask for help?

# The Decision Sequence

Use the questions below in order.

## 1. What is the task?

Identify what you want the AI tool to do.

Common tasks include:

- brainstorm ideas
- rewrite text
- summarise a document
- analyse information
- translate content
- create images
- draft a policy
- take meeting notes
- compare options
- support a decision

The risk depends on both the task and the information used.

## 2. What information are you planning to enter?

Classify the information before continuing.

Possible categories:

- public information
- fictional or synthetic information
- internal but non-confidential information
- confidential information
- personal data
- special category data
- safeguarding information
- staff or volunteer information
- donor or supporter information
- beneficiary or service-user information
- financial information
- legal information
- passwords, keys or security details

## 3. Could a person be identified?

Ask whether someone could be identified:

- directly, such as by name, email address or phone number
- indirectly, through a combination of details
- from a rare role, location, condition or incident
- by someone with background knowledge

Removing a name does not automatically make information anonymous.

## 4. Does it include personal data?

Personal data may include:

- names
- contact details
- identification numbers
- employment information
- opinions about someone
- online identifiers
- photographs
- recorded voices
- location information
- information linked to a person

If personal data is involved, pause and check the charity's policy, lawful basis, necessity, data-minimisation approach and approved-tool arrangements.

## 5. Does it include higher-risk information?

Higher-risk information may include:

- health information
- racial or ethnic origin
- political opinions
- religious or philosophical beliefs
- trade union membership
- genetic or biometric data
- sexual orientation
- criminal-offence data
- safeguarding information
- children's information
- beneficiary case notes
- disciplinary, grievance or complaint information

Treat these uses as Amber or Red unless there is a formally approved and properly governed arrangement.

## 6. Is the information confidential?

Examples include:

- unpublished board papers
- internal strategy
- contracts
- legal advice
- HR matters
- funding negotiations
- financial forecasts
- security information
- passwords and access credentials
- commercially sensitive supplier information

Confidential information may still be risky even when it is not personal data.

## 7. Has the information genuinely been anonymised?

Before treating information as anonymous, ask:

- Have all direct identifiers been removed?
- Could details still identify a person?
- Could the information be combined with other data?
- Is the person part of a small or recognisable group?
- Is the story or event distinctive?
- Has free text been checked for hidden identifiers?

Pseudonymised information is still personal data.

## 8. Is the AI tool approved for this use?

Check:

- whether the charity has approved the tool
- which product or subscription plan is being used
- whether the account is personal, free, business or enterprise
- whether prompts or outputs may be retained
- whether data may be used to improve models
- where data may be processed
- which administrative and security controls are enabled
- whether integrations can access other systems
- whether a supplier agreement or Data Processing Agreement exists

Do not assume that all versions of the same product have the same terms.

## 9. Is using this information necessary?

Ask:

- Can the task be completed without the real data?
- Can you use public information instead?
- Can you use a fictional example?
- Can you remove unnecessary detail?
- Can you summarise the issue without including identifiers?
- Can an approved internal tool perform the task instead?

Use the minimum information necessary.

## 10. Could the output affect someone?

The risk is higher if the output could influence:

- access to support
- grant eligibility
- recruitment
- employment decisions
- safeguarding actions
- risk scoring
- disciplinary decisions
- complaints
- service prioritisation
- public statements about a person
- significant decisions affecting rights or opportunities

AI should not be the sole decision-maker in high-impact situations.

## 11. Could an inaccurate or biased output cause harm?

Ask:

- Could the AI invent facts?
- Could it misrepresent someone?
- Could it reinforce bias?
- Could it create unsafe advice?
- Could it damage trust?
- Could it lead to a harmful or unfair decision?
- Could readers mistake a draft for verified guidance?

The more serious the possible harm, the stronger the human review must be.

## 12. Who must review or approve the use?

Possible reviewers include:

- line manager
- data-protection lead
- safeguarding lead
- senior leader
- trustee
- HR lead
- IT or security lead
- legal adviser
- Data Protection Officer
- procurement lead

If you are unsure, stop and ask.

# Traffic-Light Outcomes

## Green — Go ahead with normal checks

Use Green when:

- the task is low risk
- the information is public, fictional, synthetic or genuinely non-personal
- the tool is approved for the task
- no significant decision about a person is being made
- the output will receive normal human review
- limited harm is likely if the output is wrong

Examples:

- brainstorming campaign names
- rewriting already-public website copy
- summarising a public report
- creating a meeting agenda
- generating fictional examples
- improving the structure of a non-confidential document
- drafting generic social-media ideas

Green does not mean:

- the output is automatically accurate
- no human review is needed
- every AI tool is suitable
- copyright, bias or reputational risks disappear

## Amber — Pause and check

Use Amber when:

- the information is internal
- anonymisation may be incomplete
- confidentiality is involved
- the tool or subscription has not been fully assessed
- the output may influence people
- sensitive topics are involved
- the AI tool connects to workplace systems
- third-party data is involved
- retention, training or international-processing terms are unclear
- manager, safeguarding or data-protection approval may be needed

Amber means:

1. Pause.
2. Reduce the information used.
3. Check the tool and policy.
4. Seek the right approval.
5. Document the decision where appropriate.
6. Proceed only when the remaining risk is acceptable.

Examples:

- summarising internal meeting notes
- using an anonymised beneficiary story
- analysing internal survey comments
- drafting an HR process
- using an AI meeting assistant
- reviewing an internal funding bid
- uploading a confidential policy draft
- using AI-generated content in a sensitive public campaign

## Red — Stop

Use Red when the proposed use involves information or decisions that should not proceed through a general-purpose AI tool under this framework.

Examples:

- identifiable safeguarding records
- children's identifiable information
- unapproved use of health data
- beneficiary case notes
- staff grievance or disciplinary files
- donor databases
- passwords, API keys or access credentials
- legal privilege
- highly confidential board material
- significant automated decisions about individuals
- data entered contrary to policy, contract or supplier terms
- a use where foreseeable harm cannot be reduced proportionately

Red means:

- do not paste the information
- do not continue with the current workflow
- ask an appropriate specialist or decision-maker
- consider a safer alternative
- use a formally approved system only if the organisation has completed the necessary legal, security, safeguarding and governance checks

Red does not mean the activity is legally impossible in every system or circumstance. It means it should not proceed casually through a general-purpose AI tool.

# Do-Not-Paste Checklist

Do not paste the following into a general-purpose AI tool unless a formally approved arrangement exists:

- names
- personal email addresses
- phone numbers
- home addresses
- dates of birth
- identification numbers
- health records
- medical information
- safeguarding reports
- case notes
- children's information
- complaints
- staff reviews
- disciplinary information
- grievance information
- payroll data
- bank details
- donor records
- supporter databases
- beneficiary records
- passwords
- API keys
- security codes
- legal advice
- confidential board papers
- anything that could identify a vulnerable person
- anything you would not be comfortable seeing made public

# Safer Alternatives

When a task is Amber or Red, consider:

- use fictional data
- use synthetic examples
- remove direct identifiers
- remove distinctive contextual detail
- use public information
- summarise the problem without real records
- use an approved enterprise tool
- work inside an existing secure system
- ask a colleague to review the material manually
- use conventional software rather than AI
- complete a formal risk assessment or DPIA where required

# Charity-Specific Examples

## Fundraising

Green:
Brainstorm five campaign themes using public information about the charity.

Amber:
Rewrite a donor case study after careful anonymisation and approval.

Red:
Upload a donor spreadsheet containing names, contact details and giving history.

## Communications

Green:
Draft social-media captions from an already-published report.

Amber:
Use an internal communications plan containing confidential launch details.

Red:
Paste a complaint email containing identifiable personal information.

## Service Delivery

Green:
Create a fictional scenario for staff training.

Amber:
Summarise a fully anonymised service trend report.

Red:
Paste beneficiary case notes, safeguarding concerns or health information.

## Human Resources

Green:
Draft a generic interview-question bank.

Amber:
Review a non-identifiable recruitment process.

Red:
Upload CVs, grievance records, sickness information or performance reviews.

## Trustees and Governance

Green:
Create an agenda from public meeting topics.

Amber:
Summarise a confidential but non-personal policy draft using an approved tool.

Red:
Upload privileged legal advice, sensitive board papers or unredacted incident reports.

## Meetings and Transcription

Green:
Create an agenda before a meeting.

Amber:
Use a meeting transcription tool after checking consent, the tool's terms and the information likely to be discussed.

Red:
Record or upload meetings involving safeguarding cases, health details or confidential employment matters without formal approval.

# Scale This for Your Charity

## Minimum viable approach

Suitable for small charities:

- adopt the Before You Paste checklist
- name one responsible manager
- approve a small list of tools
- agree three or four clear staff rules
- escalate personal, safeguarding and confidential data
- review the framework every six months

## Structured approach

Suitable for medium-sized charities:

- maintain an approved-tools register
- maintain an AI-use or experiment register
- assess new tools before use
- provide role-based staff training
- involve safeguarding and data-protection leads
- review incidents and near misses
- review the framework quarterly

## Advanced approach

Suitable for large or complex charities:

- maintain a complete AI system inventory
- implement formal procurement and assurance
- conduct technical, legal and ethical reviews
- complete DPIAs and impact assessments
- monitor systems and outputs
- maintain audit trails
- establish multidisciplinary governance
- commission specialist or independent review where appropriate

# When to Stop and Ask for Help

Stop and seek specialist review when the task involves:

- safeguarding
- identifiable children
- health or care information
- criminal-offence data
- employment disputes
- legal privilege
- significant decisions about people
- profiling vulnerable individuals
- automated eligibility decisions
- large-scale special category data
- biometric information
- surveillance
- uncertain lawful basis
- unclear international transfers
- a possible data breach or AI incident

# Limitations

This framework is a practical first-line check.

It does not:

- determine legal compliance by itself
- replace a DPIA
- replace safeguarding procedures
- assess every AI product
- guarantee that information is anonymous
- remove the need for human judgement
- replace contractual, security or procurement review
- make high-risk AI use safe through wording alone

# Implementation Requirements

The public version of this framework should include:

- a short interactive decision tree
- a printable checklist
- Green, Amber and Red examples
- role-specific examples
- clear escalation routes
- links to relevant templates
- links to data-protection guidance
- visible version and review information
- a route to report unclear or outdated guidance

# Definition of Success

The framework succeeds when a non-technical charity worker can:

- identify the type of information they are using
- recognise obvious high-risk situations
- choose Green, Amber or Red appropriately
- reduce unnecessary data use
- know when to stop
- know who to ask
- complete a low-risk task with confidence
