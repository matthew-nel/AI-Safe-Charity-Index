# AI Safe Use Knowledge Base for UK Charities

## Editorial and Learning Design

**Version:** 1.0.0 **Status:** Foundation Document **Owner:** Matthew
Nel **Last reviewed:** July 2026 **Next review:** October 2026

# Purpose

This document defines how the knowledge base teaches, explains and
guides people.

The resource is written first for people working in small and
medium-sized UK charities who may have little or no technical
background.

# Editorial Principles

-   Write in plain UK English.
-   Explain practical tasks before technical theory.
-   Answer the reader's question within the first screen.
-   Solve one problem per page.
-   Prefer examples over abstract explanations.
-   Separate facts from recommendations.
-   Distinguish legal requirements from good practice.
-   Never assume the reader understands AI, GDPR or procurement
    terminology.

# Teaching Philosophy

People arrive with jobs to do, not topics to study.

Pages should answer questions such as:

-   Can I paste this into AI?
-   Can AI help with fundraising?
-   Can I summarise meeting notes?
-   Should volunteers use ChatGPT?

# Standard Page Structure

1.  Quick answer
2.  Traffic-light guidance
3.  What to do
4.  Worked example
5.  Why it matters
6.  Larger charity considerations
7.  Related templates
8.  Sources and review information

# Progressive Disclosure

Always provide:

## Minimum approach

Suitable for small charities.

## Structured approach

Suitable for medium-sized charities.

## Advanced approach

Suitable for larger organisations.

Advanced guidance should never dominate the primary experience.

# Traffic-light Language

## Green

Low-risk activity with normal human review.

## Amber

Pause, review and check before proceeding.

## Red

Do not continue without specialist review or an approved alternative.

Always explain why a task falls into a colour category.

# Writing Style

Use:

-   short paragraphs
-   meaningful headings
-   checklists
-   charity-specific examples
-   active voice
-   consistent terminology

Avoid:

-   unnecessary jargon
-   legalistic wording
-   unexplained acronyms
-   fear-based messaging
-   long introductions

# Examples First

Every practical page should contain:

-   one safe example
-   one unsafe example
-   one recommended workflow

Use fictional scenarios only.

# Learning Paths

Create guided routes for:

-   New to AI
-   Trustees
-   Managers
-   Communications
-   Fundraising
-   Service delivery
-   Volunteers
-   Digital teams

Each path should finish with a practical action.

# Accessibility

Design for readers with:

-   limited AI confidence
-   limited digital confidence
-   limited time

Content should be easy to scan on desktop and mobile.

Do not rely on colour alone to communicate meaning.

# Trust Signals

Every page should display:

-   Version
-   Last reviewed
-   Next review
-   Evidence level
-   Primary sources

# Editorial Checklist

Before publication confirm:

-   The audience is clear.
-   The practical answer appears first.
-   The language is understandable.
-   Examples are included.
-   Sources support the claims.
-   The next step is obvious.
-   Advanced guidance is separated appropriately.

# Definition of Success

Readers should leave every page knowing what to do next, when to stop,
and where to find deeper guidance if their organisation has more complex
governance needs.
