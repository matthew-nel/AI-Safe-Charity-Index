# Purpose

This document defines how the knowledge base should feel, read and present itself as a public resource.

It covers the overall editorial identity of the project.

The detailed speaking style and sentence-level voice are defined separately in:

- `15_TONE_OF_VOICE.md`
- `16_PLAIN_ENGLISH_STANDARD.md`
- `12_EDITORIAL_AND_LEARNING_DESIGN.md`

# Brand Position

The knowledge base should feel like:

> A calm, experienced digital colleague helping a charity make a sensible decision.

It should not feel like:

- a legal textbook
- an AI marketing website
- a regulator portal
- a corporate consultancy brochure
- a technical manual
- a sales funnel
- a generic AI-generated knowledge base

# Brand Promise

> Use AI to save time without risking people's data, trust or safety.

Every page should reinforce this promise through:

- practical advice
- clear boundaries
- realistic examples
- visible evidence
- proportionate guidance
- transparent uncertainty
- useful next steps

# Core Brand Qualities

The resource should be:

## Practical

Help readers act.

Do not publish content that explains a topic without helping someone make a decision, complete a task or know when to stop.

## Calm

Explain risk without creating panic.

Use clear warnings where needed, but avoid dramatic or fear-based language.

## Trustworthy

Show evidence, review dates, limitations and uncertainty.

Do not make confident claims that the evidence does not support.

## Human

Write like a real person helping another person.

Avoid stiff, generic and over-polished corporate language.

## Charity-first

Start from the reality of charity work.

Use examples involving:

- fundraisers
- volunteers
- trustees
- service teams
- communications staff
- small organisations with limited capacity

Do not default to enterprise technology assumptions.

## Proportionate

Give small charities the simplest sensible starting point.

Offer more advanced guidance for larger or complex organisations without making that the default.

## Evidence-led

Prioritise authoritative UK sources.

Separate:

- legal requirements
- regulatory guidance
- sector good practice
- provider facts
- project recommendations

# Editorial Identity

The project should consistently communicate:

- We understand that charities are already using AI.
- We are not here to shame people for experimenting.
- Safe use should be easier to understand.
- Small charities should not need a legal team to take sensible first steps.
- Human judgement matters.
- Good governance should be proportionate.
- Guidance should be updated when evidence changes.
- Trust matters more than hype.

# Brand Language

Prefer language such as:

- Start here
- Quick answer
- Pause and check
- Before you paste
- What this means for your charity
- A sensible first step
- What to do next
- When to ask for help
- For larger or more complex charities
- What charities told us
- Last reviewed
- Known limitations

Avoid language such as:

- Transform your charity with AI
- Unlock the power of AI
- Revolutionary
- Best in class
- Future-proof your organisation
- Leverage AI
- Seamless adoption
- Robust framework
- Enterprise-grade
- Disruptive innovation

# Editorial Hierarchy

Every page should prioritise information in this order:

1. The practical answer
2. What to do
3. The main risk
4. A realistic example
5. Why it matters
6. Deeper guidance
7. Evidence and review information

Do not begin with long background sections.

# Page Openings

Good opening:

> You can usually use AI to draft public-facing content, as long as you are not pasting personal, confidential or safeguarding information into the tool.

Weak opening:

> Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming the way modern organisations approach communications and content creation.

Pages should answer the reader's question immediately.

# Editorial Patterns

Use recurring patterns so the site feels predictable.

## Quick answer

A short response near the top.

## Traffic-light guidance

Use:

- Green — go ahead with normal checks
- Amber — pause and check
- Red — stop

Always include words as well as colour.

## What to check

Use a short checklist.

## Example

Show what safe and unsafe use looks like.

## What to do instead

Give a practical alternative.

## Scale this for your charity

Provide:

- minimum viable approach
- structured approach
- advanced approach

## Next step

End with one clear action.

# Use of Examples

Examples should be:

- fictional
- charity-specific
- realistic
- short
- easy to compare
- free from real personal data

Use examples to show:

- safe prompts
- unsafe prompts
- safer alternatives
- tool approval decisions
- staff scenarios
- trustee questions
- data-protection risks
- safeguarding red flags

Do not create examples that make complex legal or safeguarding decisions appear simple.

# Use of Green, Amber and Red

The traffic-light system is a core brand feature.

## Green

Use for low-risk uses where:

- information is public, fictional or non-personal
- the tool is approved
- normal human review is enough
- likely harm is limited

## Amber

Use for situations that require:

- a pause
- more information
- anonymisation checks
- manager review
- data-protection review
- safeguarding review
- tool approval
- stronger human oversight

## Red

Use for situations that should not proceed through a general-purpose AI tool without formal approval or specialist safeguards.

Do not overuse Red.

If everything is Red, the framework stops being useful.

# Naturalness Standard

The writing should sound like a person, not a generated content engine.

Use:

- varied sentence length
- direct verbs
- normal transitions
- specific examples
- occasional conversational phrasing
- clear admissions of uncertainty

Avoid:

- repeated sentence structures
- excessive headings with little content
- formulaic conclusions
- empty summaries
- generic motivational language
- overuse of “It is important to”
- overuse of “In today's rapidly evolving landscape”
- repeated three-part lists simply for rhythm
- unnatural enthusiasm

# Human Review Standard

Every public page should be reviewed for:

- whether it sounds natural
- whether it feels useful
- whether it sounds like Matthew James Nel's editorial judgement
- whether it avoids generic AI phrasing
- whether it is clear enough for a non-technical charity reader

Claude should not assume that polished wording is automatically good wording.

# Editorial Independence

The resource may later include:

- affiliate links
- paid services
- consultancy
- training
- sponsorship
- partner organisations

These must never influence:

- safety ratings
- evidence interpretation
- tool recommendations
- regulator summaries
- correction decisions
- research findings

Commercial relationships must be clearly disclosed.

Core safety content should remain editorially independent.

# Visual Editorial Style

The public site should feel:

- clean
- spacious
- calm
- modern
- accessible
- documentation-led
- easy to scan

Use visual elements only when they improve understanding.

Preferred elements:

- callout boxes
- decision trees
- checklists
- simple diagrams
- comparison tables
- page status labels
- icons used consistently
- print-friendly quick guides

Avoid:

- decorative clutter
- excessive colour
- novelty illustrations
- stock AI imagery
- futuristic robot visuals
- abstract technology backgrounds
- visual effects that reduce readability

# Headings

Headings should be:

- descriptive
- task-based
- written in sentence case
- easy to scan

Prefer:

- Can I upload meeting notes to AI?
- What counts as personal data?
- How to choose an AI tool
- When to ask for help

Avoid:

- Overview
- Considerations
- Key insights
- Strategic framework
- Best practice guidance

Use generic headings only where their meaning is obvious in context.

# Lists

Use lists when they make information easier to scan.

Avoid turning every paragraph into bullets.

A list should:

- group related items
- remain reasonably short
- use consistent grammar
- add clarity

Long lists should be split into sections.

# Tables

Use tables for:

- comparisons
- tool reviews
- role responsibilities
- version history
- risk levels
- template fields

Do not use tables for long prose.

Ensure tables remain understandable on mobile.

# Calls to Action

Every page should have one main next step.

Examples:

- Check your next prompt before pasting.
- Download the staff rules.
- Review your current AI tools.
- Share this page with your trustees.
- Report outdated guidance.
- Tell us what your charity needs help with.

Avoid multiple competing calls to action.

# Research and Community Tone

When asking charities for feedback:

- be clear about why it matters
- respect their time
- explain how feedback will be used
- avoid implying that every response will lead to a change
- do not pressure people to share sensitive information

Preferred wording:

> Tell us how your charity is using AI, what feels unclear and what practical guidance would help.

# Larger Charity Guidance

The core brand remains small- and medium-charity first.

Advanced guidance should use labels such as:

- For larger organisations
- For complex systems
- Advanced governance
- Specialist review

Do not allow enterprise language to dominate the main content.

# Editorial Anti-Patterns

Do not publish content that:

- sounds like a sales page
- repeats generic AI hype
- opens with broad industry commentary
- overstates certainty
- copies regulator language without interpretation
- presents recommendations as law
- uses fear to drive engagement
- assumes enterprise capacity
- treats policy as the only solution
- uses examples that are not relevant to charities
- makes every AI use sound dangerous
- makes every AI use sound beneficial
- sounds noticeably machine-generated

# Brand Review Checklist

Before publishing, confirm:

- Does this sound calm and practical?
- Does it answer the question early?
- Is the charity context clear?
- Is the main audience non-technical?
- Are the recommendations proportionate?
- Are facts separated from recommendations?
- Are examples realistic?
- Does the page avoid hype?
- Does it avoid unnecessary legal language?
- Does it sound human?
- Is advanced guidance clearly separated?
- Is creator attribution used appropriately?
- Is the next step clear?

# Definition of Success

The brand and editorial style succeeds when readers feel:

- this was written for charities like ours
- I understand the practical answer
- I know what to do next
- I know when to stop
- I trust where the guidance came from
- I do not feel talked down to
- I do not feel overwhelmed
- I can find deeper guidance if needed

The overall impression should be:

> Clear, useful, evidence-led and human.
