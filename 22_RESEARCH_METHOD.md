# Purpose

This document defines how research should be planned, gathered, assessed, analysed and converted into public guidance.

The project uses several kinds of evidence:

- regulator guidance
- legislation and government sources
- charity-sector reports
- provider documentation
- academic research
- professional guidance
- books and longform sources
- charity surveys
- interviews
- usability testing
- public feedback
- GitHub issues
- analytics and search behaviour

The method must keep these sources separate, assess their quality properly and make clear how they influence the public knowledge base.

# Core Research Principle

> Use the strongest available evidence, explain its limits and turn it into practical guidance without overstating what it proves.

# Research Goals

Research should help the project understand:

- how charities are using AI
- what risks and uncertainties they face
- where guidance is missing
- how regulation applies
- which tools are changing
- what different charity sizes need
- where staff confidence is low
- what causes friction
- what trustees need to oversee
- what guidance should be improved

# Research Types

## 1. Regulatory and Legal Research

Purpose:

- establish legal requirements
- understand regulator expectations
- identify changes in guidance
- support high-risk public claims

Priority sources:

- ICO
- UK legislation
- UK Government
- Charity Commission
- relevant national regulators
- official guidance

Rules:

- use primary sources wherever possible
- record jurisdiction
- record publication and update dates
- distinguish law from regulator interpretation
- avoid treating commentary as law
- seek specialist review for complex or contested points

## 2. Charity-Sector Research

Purpose:

- understand charity practice
- identify sector barriers
- measure confidence and adoption
- improve practical relevance

Sources may include:

- NCVO
- SCVO
- WCVA
- NICVA
- Charity Digital
- CAST
- DataKind UK
- Charity Excellence
- recognised sector networks
- charity surveys
- published charity case studies

Rules:

- note sample size and audience
- distinguish small, medium and large charities
- avoid generalising from one subsector
- record methodology where available
- identify commercial or organisational interests

## 3. Provider Research

Purpose:

- understand how AI tools handle data
- track product and plan differences
- identify changes in privacy, retention and controls
- support time-stamped tool reviews

Sources:

- provider privacy notices
- product documentation
- terms
- data-processing agreements
- security documentation
- release notes
- business and enterprise documentation

Rules:

- record exact product and plan
- record access date
- distinguish marketing claims from contractual documentation
- verify rapidly changing information regularly
- do not describe a tool as simply safe or unsafe

## 4. Academic and Professional Research

Purpose:

- provide deeper evidence
- understand harms, benefits and uncertainty
- support responsible AI, safeguarding and learning design

Rules:

- prefer peer-reviewed or recognised professional work
- record study design
- record sample and limitations
- avoid treating one study as conclusive
- check whether findings apply to UK charities
- separate theory from operational evidence

## 5. Books and Longform Research

Purpose:

- develop conceptual depth
- improve editorial thinking
- understand knowledge management, AI governance and charity practice

Rules:

- create chapter-level notes
- record edition and publication date
- distinguish author opinion from established evidence
- verify legal claims against current primary sources
- respect copyright
- avoid reproducing long extracts

## 6. Original Charity Research

Purpose:

- understand real charity use
- identify pain points
- test guidance
- improve the public resource

Methods may include:

- online surveys
- interviews
- usability testing
- workshops
- roundtables
- page feedback
- case studies
- analytics
- search-term analysis

Rules:

- define the research question first
- collect only necessary data
- use a clear privacy notice
- separate consent for follow-up
- avoid collecting sensitive case information
- publish aggregated or anonymised findings
- record limitations
- do not present small samples as representative of the whole sector

# Research Questions

Every research activity should begin with a clear question.

Examples:

- Which AI tools are small charities using?
- What stops staff from using AI confidently?
- What do trustees want to understand?
- Which parts of GDPR guidance are hardest to apply?
- How often are staff using personal AI accounts?
- What would make tool approval easier?
- Where do charities need more practical examples?

Avoid vague research goals such as:

- learn more about AI
- understand the sector
- see what people think

# Evidence Assessment

Assess each source using:

## Authority

- Who produced it?
- Are they a regulator, sector body, provider, academic or commentator?
- Do they have relevant expertise?

## Currency

- When was it published?
- When was it updated?
- Is the topic fast-moving?

## Relevance

- Does it apply to UK charities?
- Does it apply to the audience or workflow?
- Does it apply to the product or plan being discussed?

## Method

- How was the evidence produced?
- Is the methodology visible?
- Is the sample suitable?
- Are there obvious biases?

## Independence

- Is the source selling something?
- Is it promoting a product?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed?

## Limitations

- What does the source not prove?
- What remains uncertain?
- What context may not transfer?

# Research Confidence

Use:

## High Confidence

Use when:

- primary authority is clear
- multiple strong sources agree
- evidence is current
- relevance is direct

## Moderate Confidence

Use when:

- evidence is credible but limited
- practice is evolving
- sources are consistent but not definitive

## Low Confidence

Use when:

- evidence is sparse
- sources conflict
- the source is old
- the context is only partly relevant

## Unverified

Use when:

- a claim has not been checked
- the source is missing
- current information cannot be confirmed

# Separating Evidence Types

Always label:

- legal requirement
- regulator guidance
- charity-sector evidence
- provider fact
- original research finding
- playbook recommendation
- editorial interpretation

Do not merge these into one undifferentiated conclusion.

# Research Synthesis

When reviewing multiple sources:

1. identify what they agree on
2. identify differences
3. compare evidence strength
4. compare jurisdiction and date
5. identify repeated themes
6. record gaps
7. explain uncertainty
8. derive practical implications
9. separate findings from recommendations

# Original Research Analysis

For surveys:

- report sample size
- report date range
- report charity-size distribution
- report role distribution
- explain recruitment method
- distinguish closed and open questions
- avoid false precision
- include limitations
- use quotes only with permission and suitable anonymisation

For interviews:

- summarise themes
- avoid exposing identities
- distinguish frequent from isolated themes
- record contradictions
- do not overstate generalisability

For usability testing:

- record task
- record participant type
- identify observed problems
- separate observation from opinion
- prioritise repeated or high-risk issues

# Triangulation

Where possible, combine:

- authoritative guidance
- sector evidence
- original charity research
- public feedback
- observed site behaviour

Example:

A new page may be justified when:

- regulator guidance establishes the issue
- sector reports show a capability gap
- charities repeatedly ask the same question
- site searches show users cannot find the answer

# Research Ethics

Research should:

- respect participants
- minimise data collection
- avoid harm
- avoid unnecessary sensitive questions
- make participation optional
- explain how data will be used
- allow withdrawal where appropriate
- separate research from sales
- protect confidentiality
- report findings honestly

# Research and Commercial Independence

Commercial opportunities must not shape findings.

If the project later offers:

- consultancy
- training
- paid templates
- affiliate links
- sponsorship

Then:

- disclose relevant interests
- keep research analysis independent
- do not suppress negative findings
- do not change tool ratings for commercial benefit
- separate commercial contact consent from research participation

# From Research to Guidance

Research should move through this sequence:

```text
Research question
      ↓
Source gathering
      ↓
Evidence assessment
      ↓
Synthesis
      ↓
Claim review
      ↓
Editorial interpretation
      ↓
Draft guidance
      ↓
Human review
      ↓
Publication
      ↓
Feedback and re-evaluation
```

# Publication Threshold

Do not publish a material claim unless:

- the source is recorded
- the claim is recorded
- the evidence type is clear
- limitations are understood
- the wording matches the evidence
- uncertainty is visible
- high-risk content has appropriate review

# Monthly Research Prompt

Use:

```text
Review the new research material added to the Claude Project.

For each source or research output:

1. Identify the research question.
2. Classify the evidence type.
3. Assess authority, currency, relevance, method, independence and limitations.
4. Assign a confidence level.
5. Separate facts, findings, recommendations and interpretation.
6. Compare with existing evidence.
7. Identify claims affected.
8. Identify public pages affected.
9. Identify evidence gaps.
10. Recommend no change, monitoring, clarification, new guidance or specialist review.

Produce:

- source summaries
- research synthesis
- claim-register updates
- page impact report
- further research questions
- publication recommendations

Do not overstate evidence.
Do not treat a small or self-selecting sample as representative.
Do not present provider marketing as independent evidence.
```

# Quality Checklist

Before accepting a research output, confirm:

- Is the research question clear?
- Is the method appropriate?
- Is the source quality assessed?
- Is the sample described?
- Are limitations visible?
- Are findings separated from recommendations?
- Is the charity context clear?
- Are claims traceable?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed?
- Is personal data protected?
- Is the conclusion proportionate?

# Definition of Success

The research method succeeds when the project can:

- use different evidence types appropriately
- avoid overstating findings
- combine regulation, sector evidence and charity experience
- identify what is known and unknown
- improve guidance through real research
- maintain public trust
- produce practical recommendations that remain traceable to evidence

The research system should be rigorous enough to earn trust and simple enough to maintain every month.
