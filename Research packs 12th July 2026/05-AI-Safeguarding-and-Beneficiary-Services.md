# AI Safeguarding and Beneficiary Services

## Executive summary

Evidence from UK mental-health charities, professional bodies and parliamentary research highlights that AI tools and chatbots are increasingly used by the public for mental-health support, self-help and wellbeing, but that these uses carry significant risks of harm, misinformation, bias, exclusion and privacy breaches, particularly for children and adults at risk.[web:81][web:82][web:83][web:88][web:90][web:91][web:92] While some AI applications may offer administrative support or low-risk assistance in beneficiary services (e.g. translation of non-sensitive information, summarising anonymised feedback, or internal triage support), current guidance warns against relying on general-purpose chatbots for crisis support, clinical advice or safeguarding decisions.

For UK charities delivering advice, crisis, mental-health, domestic-abuse, children’s and adult-at-risk services, AI deployment must be cautious, proportionate and grounded in safeguarding and clinical governance. Many uses require DPIAs, specialist review, robust testing, monitoring and clear escalation pathways to trained staff.[web:51][web:69][web:84][web:91] Some high-risk uses—such as AI-driven diagnosis, automated triage of crisis contacts, or AI-generated advice in one-to-one support—should generally be avoided in their current form.

## Scope

This pack examines AI in:

- Beneficiary services and advice.
- Crisis, mental-health and domestic-abuse support.
- Children’s services and services for adults at risk.
- Health-related services and casework.
- Public chatbots, triage, translation, meeting and call transcription.
- Summarising case notes, generating advice, monitoring conversations and content moderation.

It focuses on risks and possible benefits for UK charities, drawing on mental-health and safeguarding evidence, digital health research and charity governance guidance.[web:81][web:83][web:88][web:91][web:51][web:69] Legal details are referenced at a high level; this pack is not legal advice.

## Research questions

1. What AI uses are emerging in beneficiary, advice and safeguarding services, and how widespread are they?
2. What risks arise from AI in these contexts, including hallucinations, unsafe advice, failure to recognise emergencies, automation bias, confidentiality, special-category data, inferred sensitivity, bias, exclusion and digital poverty?
3. Which AI uses may offer administrative or low-risk support, and which should be avoided or heavily constrained?
4. When are DPIAs, specialist review and board or senior-leadership oversight appropriate for beneficiary-facing AI?
5. How can charities design proportionate safeguards for vulnerable people when considering AI tools?

### Evidence hierarchy and limitations

Regulator and parliamentary sources are treated as primary; charity and mental-health sector analyses provide applied context.[web:84][web:91][web:51][web:88] There is limited published evidence on AI use directly within UK charity safeguarding services; much evidence concerns public use of AI tools for mental health or digital health apps more broadly.[web:81][web:82][web:90][web:92]

## Key findings

1. General-purpose AI chatbots are widely used by the public for mental-health support, but professional bodies and charities warn that these tools can be inaccurate, unsafe and harmful, particularly for vulnerable users.[web:81][web:82][web:83][web:90][web:91][web:88]
2. Documented severe harms in AI mental-health contexts have primarily involved unintended uses of general companion chatbots, not clinically governed systems.[web:91]
3. AI tools can support administrative tasks in services (e.g. summarising anonymised feedback, securely transcribing training content) but should not be relied upon for clinical or safeguarding decisions.[web:91][web:88][web:51]
4. AI in mental-health and safeguarding contexts raises complex ethical and regulatory questions around data protection, bias, accountability and patient safety; evidence and regulation are still evolving.[web:84][web:91][web:88]

## Detailed research

### Public and beneficiary use of AI mental-health tools

Mental Health UK’s research finds that **37% of UK adults** have used an AI chatbot to support their mental health or wellbeing, reflecting rapid uptake of AI tools beyond formal services.[web:82][web:90] Motivations include ease of access and long NHS waiting times, particularly around festive periods when mental health issues may intensify.[web:90]

Professional bodies warn of significant risks:

- BACP reports therapists’ concerns about children turning to AI for mental-health advice, highlighting dangers from inaccurate advice and the absence of human understanding.[web:85]
- NHS England’s national medical director for mental health warns that AI chatbots cannot fully understand nuanced serious mental-health situations, may agree with problematic thoughts and can give completely wrong advice, especially for those experiencing psychosis.[web:90]

The UK Parliamentary POSTnote on AI and mental healthcare notes that all severe harms identified in the research were associated with **unintended uses of general companion chatbots**, not dedicated NHS tools.[web:91] Ethical concerns include harm, bias, privacy, transparency, accountability, and the risk that AI may exclude some service users while increasing accessibility for others.[web:91]

### AI in beneficiary services: possible support roles

Charities may consider AI for:

- **Administrative support:** summarising anonymised feedback, generating internal reports, drafting non-clinical information materials.[web:88][web:91]
- **Translation:** translating non-identifiable information leaflets to multiple languages to improve accessibility.[web:89]
- **Internal triage support:** helping staff classify non-urgent queries or route questions to appropriate teams, under human supervision.[web:69][web:84]

In these roles, AI handles low-risk content, with human review ensuring accuracy and appropriateness. Even here, DPIA screening, data minimisation and clear boundaries are important.

### High-risk AI uses in safeguarding and beneficiary services

High-risk uses include:

- **Direct mental-health advice:** AI-generated responses to individuals seeking mental-health or crisis support; evidence shows risks from inaccurate, unsafe advice and failure to recognise emergencies.[web:81][web:83][web:90][web:91]
- **Automated triage of crisis contacts:** AI systems assigning urgency without human review, which risks missed emergencies and serious harm.[web:90][web:88]
- **Decision-making based on case notes:** AI systems making or materially influencing decisions about support, risk level or eligibility based on case data.[web:84][web:91]

Current evidence and professional guidance strongly caution against these uses in charity safeguarding and beneficiary services.

### Special-category data, confidentiality and privacy

AI in safeguarding and beneficiary services inherently touches special-category data (health, mental health, sexuality, religious beliefs) and sometimes criminal-offence data (abuse, offending). UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 impose stringent requirements for processing such data, including explicit or other appropriate lawful bases and additional conditions.[web:84][web:91]

General-purpose AI tools often lack robust contractual safeguards and clear data-handling guarantees. Using them with sensitive beneficiary data risks breaches of confidentiality and data protection, especially where tools retain or train on inputs.

### Bias, discrimination and exclusion

Evidence shows that large language models can absorb and reproduce structural discrimination, reinforcing stereotypes or invalidating certain identities, particularly in mental-health contexts.[web:88][web:91] AI tools may also exacerbate digital inequality: those with better access, literacy and resources benefit, while vulnerable groups face higher risks.[web:88][web:91]

### Oversight, testing and incident response

Centre for Mental Health and the POSTnote highlight limited regulation and evidence for many AI mental-health tools, and call for stronger oversight, testing and patient-centred governance.[web:88][web:91][web:92] Charities must therefore implement their own safeguards and incident-response processes, even in the absence of detailed sector-specific AI regulation.

## Implications by charity size

- **Micro and small charities:** Should generally avoid direct AI interaction with beneficiaries in safeguarding, crisis and mental-health contexts; focus AI on non-sensitive administrative support and information materials, with clear prohibitions on entering case data or using chatbots for advice.[web:81][web:83][web:90]
- **Medium and large charities:** May explore carefully scoped AI support (e.g. translation, anonymised feedback analysis) but require DPIAs, specialist safeguarding and clinical input, and robust governance; high-risk uses should be avoided or subject to rigorous scrutiny.[web:88][web:91][web:51]

## Practical implications

- AI in safeguarding and beneficiary services is primarily a risk topic, not a short-term efficiency opportunity; any benefits must be weighed against credible harms.
- Administrative and low-risk support uses are possible, but direct interaction with vulnerable beneficiaries via general-purpose AI tools is currently high risk.

## Recommended controls

### People and accountability

- Involve safeguarding leads, clinical professionals and trustees in any AI decisions that affect beneficiary services.[web:51][web:88]

### Policies and processes

- Explicitly prohibit entering identifiable safeguarding case data or mental-health details into general-purpose AI tools.
- Restrict AI chatbots to clearly labelled, non-advice signposting roles, if used at all.

### Data protection and DPIAs

- Conduct DPIA screening for any AI system involving special-category data, children or adults at risk.

### Safeguarding

- Maintain clear escalation pathways to trained humans; AI must not be the only resource for people in distress.

### Monitoring and incident response

- Monitor AI use and responses; treat harms or near misses as serious incidents requiring review and possible regulator engagement.

## Proportionate implementation

- **Minimum practice:** Prohibit high-risk AI uses in safeguarding and beneficiary contexts; use AI only for non-sensitive, internal support; ensure staff understand risks.
- **Stronger practice:** Implement DPIAs, governance frameworks and training for any AI considered for beneficiary services.

## Questions charities should ask

- Are vulnerable beneficiaries directly interacting with AI, and if so, why?
- What evidence supports the safety of the AI tool we are considering?
- How will we ensure immediate human support and robust incident response?

## Evidence gaps and uncertainties

- Limited charity-specific data on AI use and harms in safeguarding services.
- Rapidly evolving AI tools and regulatory responses; this pack should be reviewed regularly.

## Conclusions

Given current evidence, UK charities should treat AI in safeguarding, advice and beneficiary services with extreme caution. Some administrative and low-risk support uses are possible, but direct AI interaction with vulnerable people for advice, crisis or mental-health support carries documented risks and should generally be avoided or tightly constrained.
