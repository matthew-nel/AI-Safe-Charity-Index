# Trustee and Leadership Oversight

## Executive summary

Recent UK charity-sector research highlights a growing gap between widespread, day-to-day AI use and limited board-level oversight.[web:51][web:200][web:202][web:197][web:201] Charity Digital Skills survey data shows rapid increases in AI use across charities (from around 61% in 2024 to 76% in 2025 and 88% in 2026), yet Charity Commission survey data and trustee-focused analyses report that only a small fraction of trustees say their charity is using AI.[web:200][web:202] Trustees remain legally responsible for risks arising from AI, including data protection compliance, safeguarding, bias and discrimination, financial controls, procurement, cybersecurity, public trust and serious-incident reporting.[web:51][web:195][web:196]

This pack synthesises Charity Commission statements, governance guidance, sector reports and trustee-facing resources to set out practical oversight responsibilities, assurance questions and a one-page trustee AI dashboard sized for different charity capacities.[web:51][web:46][web:71][web:113][web:195] It emphasises that trustees do not need to be AI experts, but must treat AI as a cross-cutting governance issue, ensure proportionate policies and controls, and insist on clear accountability for AI-assisted decisions that affect beneficiaries, donors, staff and volunteers.

## Scope

This pack covers trustee and senior-leadership oversight of AI in UK charities, including:

- Trustee duties, collective decision-making and delegation.
- Risk ownership and accountability for AI-assisted decisions.
- Data protection, safeguarding, cybersecurity, equality and discrimination.
- Financial controls, procurement and supplier due diligence.
- Reputational risk, public trust and serious-incident reporting.
- Shadow AI, staff and volunteer use.
- Policy approval, risk appetite, assurance reporting, incident review and internal audit.

It provides a practical assurance framework and a one-page dashboard suitable for micro, small, medium and large charities.[web:46][web:71][web:195][web:201]

## Research questions

1. What do UK charity regulators and governance codes expect from trustees regarding AI use and risk?[web:51][web:71][web:203]
2. How widespread is AI use compared with trustee awareness, and what governance gaps have been identified?[web:200][web:202][web:197][web:201]
3. What oversight is proportionate for different charity sizes and risk profiles?
4. How should trustees integrate AI into existing governance structures, risk management and serious-incident reporting?
5. What practical questions and dashboard measures can trustees use to assure themselves that AI risks are being managed?

Sources include:

- Charity Commission trustee survey data on AI use.[web:200]
- Charity Governance Code 2025 update summaries and commentary, including references to technology and AI policies as good governance indicators.[web:71][web:202]

### Evidence hierarchy and limitations

Charity Commission documents and governance codes are treated as primary for trustee duties.[web:51][web:203][web:71] Sector surveys and commentary provide insight but are self-reported and may over-represent digitally engaged charities.[web:200][web:202][web:197] Professional articles interpret duties and are not regulators; their recommendations are treated as good practice rather than legal requirements.[web:196][web:199]

## Key findings

1. Trustees remain legally responsible for AI-related risks, including data protection, safeguarding, equality, financial controls and reputational risk, regardless of whether they are personally aware of AI use in their charity.[web:51][web:195][web:196]
2. There is a documented governance gap: AI is widely used across charity operations, yet many boards lack AI strategies, policies or structured oversight.[web:200][web:202][web:197][web:201]
3. The 2025 Charity Governance Code refresh and sector guidance explicitly call for boards to consider technology and AI as part of governance, including having a technology/AI policy as evidence of good practice.[web:71][web:202]
4. Effective AI oversight can be proportionately integrated into existing structures (risk registers, policies, audit, safeguarding and data protection) rather than requiring separate AI committees in most charities.[web:46][web:196][web:199]

## Detailed research

### Charity Commission perspective

The Charity Commission’s blog on AI (April 2024) stresses that trustees must consider both opportunities and risks presented by AI, including data security, copyright, misinformation and reputational damage, and apply existing guidance on trustee duties and risk management rather than expecting separate AI rules.[web:51][web:203] The Commission notes that AI can free up time, but emphasises responsible use that furthers charitable purposes and aligns with trustees’ duty of care, prudence and compliance with the law.[web:51]

Commission survey data released in August 2024 shows that only **3% of trustees** reported their charity using AI, rising to **8%** among larger charities (income over £1m).[web:200] Among those, AI was mainly used for drafting web content (58%), generating internal documents (39%) and some fundraising (24%).[web:200] This low reported usage contrasts with broader sector surveys pointing to much higher day-to-day use, highlighting a gap in board awareness.[web:202]

### Governance codes and trustee guidance

GovernanceAI and Third Sector AI report that the 2025 refresh of the Charity Governance Code explicitly references technology and AI risks, with a technology and AI policy cited as evidence of good governance.[web:71][web:202] Trustees are expected to ensure that board decisions take account of digital and AI risks, that organisational policies cover AI use, and that training is available for trustees where needed.[web:71][web:202]

Plinth’s “AI Strategy for Charity Trustees” frames AI as a strategic governance issue and recommends that boards ask what AI is used for, who is accountable, which data is affected, and how vulnerable people are protected.[web:46] Charity Excellence’s report “From Quiet Use to Strategic Leadership” concludes that strategy, accountability and training around AI are often “red” across charities, meaning boards have yet to take meaningful action.[web:4][web:195][web:197]

### Documented governance gap

Third Sector AI analysis summarises Charity Digital Skills survey findings showing a rapid increase in AI use (61% in 2024, 76% in 2025, 88% in 2026) alongside weak AI governance: only 16% of charities had an AI policy in 2024, rising to 48% in 2025 and 60% having a policy in place or in development by 2026.[web:202][web:113] Many of these policies are described as superficial, lacking clear scope, prohibitions and accountability.[web:113]

Curia AI and Lubbock Fine articles highlight that trustees remain legally responsible even when AI use is informal or “shadow”, and warn that unmanaged AI undermines public trust, especially where AI-generated imagery or profiling is used without clear safeguards.[web:195][web:201] Charity Excellence stresses that AI governance is a structural challenge, not a technical one; boards must take reasonable steps to understand AI use and ensure appropriate governance, even without being AI experts.[web:195][web:197]

### Trustee duties and AI

Standard trustee duties under charity law include acting in the best interests of the charity, managing resources responsibly, ensuring compliance with laws and regulations, and avoiding undue risk to beneficiaries and public trust.[web:51][web:196] AI-related implications include:

- **Data protection:** Trustees must ensure UK GDPR and Data Protection Act compliance for AI tools handling personal data.[web:111][web:51]
- **Safeguarding:** Trustees must ensure AI does not undermine safeguarding arrangements or deliver unsafe advice to beneficiaries.[web:51][web:88]
- **Equality and discrimination:** AI-driven profiling or decision-making must not produce discriminatory outcomes contrary to Equality Act duties.[web:50][web:69]
- **Financial controls:** AI procurement, licensing and usage must be subject to appropriate authorisation and financial oversight.[web:196]
- **Cybersecurity:** AI integrations and connectors can introduce security and confidentiality risks; trustees must ensure these are managed.[web:86][web:196]

## Trustee AI assurance framework

A practical assurance framework can be built around key questions:

- What AI systems are being used (including consumer tools and embedded features)?[web:201][web:202]
- Why are they being used, and what decisions or outputs do they influence?[web:46]
- Who approved them, and under which policy or process?[web:113]
- What information can be entered (and what is prohibited), especially in relation to personal and special-category data?[web:51][web:111]
- Are consumer accounts being used for charity work (shadow AI)?[web:201]
- Are systems connected to internal data (SharePoint, Drive, CRM), and how are permissions controlled?[web:128][web:148]
- Are beneficiaries, donors, children or adults at risk affected, and how is safeguarding integrated?[web:88][web:69]
- Is AI making or materially influencing decisions about people, and what human oversight exists?[web:91][web:196]
- Can individuals challenge AI-assisted decisions or request human review?[web:51][web:111]
- What incidents or near misses have occurred, and how are they recorded and reviewed?[web:88][web:197]

## One-page trustee AI assurance dashboard

### Recommended measures

- Existence and quality of an AI policy (scope, prohibitions, approvals, data protection, safeguarding).[web:113][web:71]
- AI use register (systems, purposes, data types, approvals, owners).[web:201]
- DPIA screening and full DPIAs for high-risk uses (profiling, beneficiary-facing tools).[web:111][web:84]
- Training completion for staff and trustees on AI policy and risks.[web:202]
- Incident and near-miss log entries related to AI.[web:88][web:197]

### Risk indicators

- AI use without board-approved policy.
- Shadow AI (consumer tools used by staff without approval).[web:201]
- AI tools connected to internal data with weak permission controls.[web:128][web:148]
- AI influencing decisions about vulnerable beneficiaries or donors without DPIA and specialist review.[web:88][web:91]

### Escalation thresholds

- Any AI-related data breach or safeguarding incident.
- AI outputs leading to discriminatory or harmful outcomes.
- Significant public complaint or media coverage related to AI use.

### Reporting frequency and ownership

- Quarterly AI governance report to trustees (policy status, register, incidents, training).[web:71][web:196]
- Named executive lead for AI governance; trustee or committee with oversight.[web:195][web:46]

## Implications by charity size

### Micro and volunteer-led charities

- Focus on awareness and a simple AI policy, basic register, and clear prohibitions on entering sensitive data into consumer tools.[web:33][web:51]
- Oversight can sit with the chair or a named trustee; assurance via annual review and incident reporting.

### Small charities

- Require a more detailed AI policy, a simple approval process, and explicit integration with data protection and safeguarding.[web:71][web:113]
- Trustees should receive at least annual updates and review incidents and DPIA screenings.

### Medium-sized charities

- Need structured AI governance: dedicated AI use register, formal approvals, DPIA processes, procurement checks and internal audit.[web:196][web:201]
- Boards may assign an AI or digital sub-committee, with regular reporting.

### Large and complex charities

- Require board-level AI strategy, integration with enterprise risk management, detailed oversight of connectors and automated decision systems, and specialist safeguarding and data protection input.[web:4][web:196][web:195]

## Practical implications

Trustees should treat AI governance as part of existing duties rather than an optional extra. Minimal oversight (policy, register, prohibitions, incident log) is proportionate for small organisations; larger charities need more formal frameworks, but all boards must know what AI is used for and how risks are controlled.[web:51][web:71][web:201]

## Recommended controls

- People: Named AI governance lead, trustee oversight, training for staff and trustees.[web:46][web:202]
- Policy and process: Board-approved AI policy, AI use register, DPIA screening, incident and near-miss logging.[web:113][web:111]
- Information governance: Data minimisation rules for AI, lawful basis checks, clear prohibitions on sensitive data in general-purpose tools.[web:51][web:111]
- Safeguarding: Specialist review for any AI interacting with beneficiaries, children or adults at risk; explicit bans on AI advice in crisis contexts.[web:88][web:91]
- Supplier assurance: Due diligence on DPAs, training on customer data, retention, security and audit controls.[web:128][web:160][web:193]
- Technical controls: Organisational accounts, SSO, logging, connector permissions, data-loss prevention.[web:128][web:148][web:162]

## Proportionate implementation

- Minimum practice: AI policy, register, simple approval, incident logging, named lead; suitable for micro and small charities.[web:33][web:71]
- Stronger practice: Integrated risk management, DPIAs, procurement checks, internal audit; suitable for medium and large charities.[web:196][web:201]

## Questions trustees should ask

- What AI tools are in use, and who approved them?[web:201]
- What data do these tools access, and how is it protected?[web:111][web:128]
- How are beneficiaries, donors and staff affected, and can they challenge AI-assisted outcomes?[web:51][web:88]

## Evidence gaps and uncertainties

- Limited regulator-specific AI guidance for charities; trustees must apply general duties to AI.[web:51][web:203]
- Limited published data on AI incidents in charities; boards should treat near misses as learning opportunities.

## Conclusions

AI is widely used in UK charities, often informally, while trustee oversight and formal governance lag behind. Trustees do not need to be AI specialists but must ensure proportionate policies, registers, approvals, DPIAs and incident review processes are in place, aligned with their existing duties.[web:51][web:200][web:202][web:195][web:196]
