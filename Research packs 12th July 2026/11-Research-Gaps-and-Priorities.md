# Research Gaps and Priorities

## Executive summary

Current research shows rapid growth in AI use across UK charities, uneven governance and conditional public trust, but significant gaps remain in evidence about beneficiaries’ views, safeguarding impacts, long-term organisational effects and charity-specific incidents.[web:259][web:12][web:265][web:197][web:261] Sector reports highlight widespread informal AI use for drafting bids and communications, analysis and workload management, yet very limited public data on how AI affects beneficiaries, staff decision-making or equality in access to support.[web:4][web:197][web:69][web:264]

This pack identifies key research gaps—including charity-size-specific adoption data, long-term organisational impacts, shadow AI, volunteer use, connectors and data-sharing, environmental impacts and accessibility—and proposes research questions, methods and a six- and twelve-month roadmap for the AI Safe Use Playbook.

## Scope

Areas covered:

- Adoption and governance by charity size.
- Beneficiary and public attitudes (including children and adults at risk).
- Charity-specific AI incidents and near misses.
- Workplace AI controls and connectors.
- Long-term organisational impacts (culture, decision-making, trust).
- Volunteer and shadow AI use.
- Environmental impact and accessibility.
- Procurement capacity and governance interventions.

## Method

### Sources

- Charity Excellence Future Charity report (AI in the charity sector 2026).[web:4][web:259][web:197]
- Charity Digital Skills Report AI findings.[web:12][web:265]
- CharityTracker public-attitude research on AI in charities.[web:261][web:262]
- Saltways/UEA research on AI-generated imagery.[web:260][web:264]
- Recognising Excellence and other sector AI reports.[web:2][web:263]

### Limitations

Sector studies rely mainly on self-reported surveys of charities and public attitudes; charity-specific incidents and internal decision-making impacts are under-documented.[web:259][web:12][web:261]

## Key gaps

### 1. Charity-size-specific adoption and governance

**What is not known:** Detailed, comparable data on AI use, governance maturity and risk by micro, small, medium and large charities.[web:12][web:265][web:197]

**Why it matters:** Governance and capacity differ markedly by size; proportionate guidance requires understanding actual practice and constraints.[web:33][web:4]

**Existing evidence:** Charity Digital Skills and Charity Excellence reports give high-level adoption and governance figures but do not provide detailed breakdowns by income band and activity type.[web:12][web:265][web:197]

**Recommended question:** How does AI adoption, governance and risk vary across UK charity sizes and sub-sectors?

**Method:** Sector-wide survey with stratified sampling by income band and area of work; qualitative interviews in each size category.

**Priority level:** High (6–12 months).

### 2. Beneficiaries’ views, especially children and adults at risk

**What is not known:** Systematic evidence on how beneficiaries, children and adults at risk experience or perceive AI use in charity services.[web:88][web:91][web:266]

**Why it matters:** Safeguarding and trust depend on lived experience; guidance must reflect beneficiary perspectives.[web:69][web:261]

**Existing evidence:** Digital mental-health studies and CharityTracker public-attitude research show conditional acceptance of AI and strong discomfort with AI influencing decisions about who receives help.[web:91][web:261][web:266] However, these are general public samples, not charity service users.

**Recommended question:** How do beneficiaries, including children and adults at risk, perceive and experience AI use in charity services?

**Method:** Participatory research with beneficiaries, focus groups and interviews, with strong ethical safeguards.

**Priority level:** High (6–12 months).

### 3. Charity-specific AI incidents and near misses

**What is not known:** Documented, sector-wide data on AI incidents (e.g. data leaks, unsafe advice, biased profiling) within charities.[web:88][web:259]

**Why it matters:** Incident learning drives proportionate controls and serious-incident reporting guidance.[web:51][web:196]

**Existing evidence:** Healthcare and public-service incidents are better documented; charity-sector insights are sparse and anecdotal.[web:205][web:211][web:212][web:88]

**Recommended question:** What AI incidents and near misses have charities experienced, and what root causes and responses are common?

**Method:** Anonymous reporting survey, incident case-study collection, collaboration with regulators and sector bodies.

**Priority level:** High (6–12 months).

### 4. Independent testing of workplace AI controls and connectors

**What is not known:** Independent evaluations of whether enterprise AI products (e.g. Copilot, Gemini, ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude) behave as advertised in charity contexts—especially connectors, permission inheritance and retention.[web:128][web:148][web:160][web:193]

**Why it matters:** Vendor claims are not independent evidence; charities rely on controls to protect sensitive data.[web:243][web:252]

**Existing evidence:** Vendor docs describe controls; professional analyses highlight potential gaps but not charity-specific testing.[web:128][web:126][web:192]

**Recommended question:** How do workplace AI tools handle charity data in practice, including connectors, permissions, retention and training?

**Method:** Technical testing in controlled charity environments, red-team exercises focusing on connectors, oversharing and retention.

**Priority level:** High (12 months).

### 5. Long-term organisational effects

**What is not known:** How sustained AI use affects charity culture, decision-making, staff roles, bias and accountability over time.[web:10][web:4]

**Why it matters:** Governance guidance must anticipate long-term impacts beyond initial efficiency gains.[web:69][web:216]

**Existing evidence:** Commentaries note potential shifts but lack longitudinal studies; some public-sector work explores human–AI interactions and automation bias.[web:212][web:216]

**Recommended question:** How does AI adoption change decision-making, accountability and organisational culture in charities over several years?

**Method:** Longitudinal case studies, repeated interviews, governance document analysis.

**Priority level:** Medium (12+ months).

### 6. Volunteer and shadow AI use

**What is not known:** Extent and nature of AI use by volunteers and staff using consumer tools outside formal governance.[web:197][web:201]

**Why it matters:** Shadow AI is a major risk route; governance must reflect actual practice.[web:259][web:12]

**Existing evidence:** Sector surveys reference “informal use” but do not detail volunteer or shadow patterns.[web:197][web:265]

**Recommended question:** How do volunteers and staff use AI informally, and what risks and opportunities arise?

**Method:** Anonymous surveys, focus groups, ethnographic observation in selected charities.

**Priority level:** High (6–12 months).

### 7. Public trust and synthetic media

**What is not known:** Detailed charity-specific evidence on how AI-generated imagery, video and synthetic beneficiary stories affect public trust and donor behaviour.[web:260][web:264][web:261]

**Why it matters:** Visual storytelling is central to fundraising; synthetic media may undermine authenticity and trust.[web:260][web:261]

**Existing evidence:** Saltways/UEA research and CharityTracker polling show sensitivity around synthetic imagery and AI decisions about support, but not extensive fundraising experiments.[web:260][web:264][web:261]

**Recommended question:** How do AI-generated images and synthetic beneficiary stories impact public trust and fundraising outcomes for charities?

**Method:** Controlled A/B testing of campaigns, public surveys and qualitative interviews, with strict ethical safeguards.

**Priority level:** Medium (12 months).

### 8. Accessibility and digital poverty

**What is not known:** How AI-enabled services interact with accessibility needs, digital literacy and poverty among beneficiaries and volunteers.[web:88][web:266]

**Why it matters:** AI may both assist and exclude; guidance must avoid deepening inequalities.[web:69][web:261]

**Existing evidence:** Mental-health and digital-inequality literature suggests unequal benefits and harms but not charity-specific patterns.[web:88][web:266]

**Recommended question:** How do AI tools affect accessibility and digital inclusion in charity services?

**Method:** Surveys and qualitative work with beneficiaries experiencing digital poverty or accessibility challenges.

**Priority level:** Medium (12+ months).

### 9. Environmental impact

**What is not known:** Charity-specific evidence on the environmental impact of AI use and how to integrate sustainability into AI governance.[web:4][web:10]

**Why it matters:** Environmental stewardship is part of many charities’ missions; AI workloads may conflict with sustainability goals.[web:4]

**Existing evidence:** Global analyses of AI energy use; limited charity-specific work.[web:243]

**Recommended question:** What is the environmental footprint of AI use in charities, and how can it be managed responsibly?

**Method:** Estimation of AI-related energy use, integration into sustainability reporting and governance frameworks.

**Priority level:** Low–medium (12+ months).

### 10. Effectiveness of governance interventions

**What is not known:** Empirical evidence on which AI governance interventions (policies, registers, training, DPIAs) most effectively reduce risk in charities.[web:197][web:201]

**Why it matters:** Guidance should focus on interventions with demonstrated impact, not just compliance forms.[web:51][web:196]

**Existing evidence:** Surveys describe governance gaps; few evaluations of intervention outcomes.

**Recommended question:** Which governance and training interventions measurably improve AI safety and trust in charities?

**Method:** Action-research projects testing interventions in partner charities, with pre/post measures.

**Priority level:** High (12 months).

## Six-month research roadmap

1. **Size-specific adoption and governance survey** (Gap 1).
2. **Volunteer and shadow AI use study** (Gap 6).
3. **Beneficiary perception scoping study** with ethical design (Gap 2).
4. **Incident and near-miss anonymous reporting pilot** (Gap 3).
5. **Public trust and AI conditional-licence synthesis** using CharityTracker data and existing polls.[web:261][web:259]

## Twelve-month research roadmap

1. **Independent testing of workplace AI connectors and controls** in selected charities (Gap 4).[web:128][web:148][web:160]
2. **Longitudinal organisational impact case studies** (Gap 5).
3. **Synthetic media and fundraising experiments with ethical safeguards** (Gap 7).[web:260][web:264]
4. **Accessibility and digital poverty study** (Gap 8).[web:88][web:266]
5. **Governance intervention evaluation projects** (Gap 10).
6. **Environmental impact scoping and guidance** (Gap 9).
