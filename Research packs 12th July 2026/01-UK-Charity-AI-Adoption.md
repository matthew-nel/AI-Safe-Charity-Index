# UK Charity AI Adoption 2025026

## Executive summary

Recent UK research indicates that around three-quarters of charities are now using AI tools in some form, but most remain at early, exploratory stages and lack robust governance, policies and skills.[web:1][web:12][web:9] Large and infrastructure charities are more likely to use AI, have begun developing policies and strategies, and are actively experimenting with organisational use, while many small and micro organisations are experimenting informally with consumer tools and have limited confidence in responsible use.[web:1][web:12][web:9]

Across multiple surveys, charities report strong interest in AIparticularly for administration, fundraising copy, grant prospecting and content creationbut also significant concerns about data protection, privacy, bias, discrimination, factual accuracy and the ethical implications of AI in services.[web:1][web:12][web:5][web:9] Skills and governance gaps are pronounced: many boards and chief executives rate their AI skills as poor, few charities have a comprehensive AI strategy, and only a minority have developed AI policies, acceptable-use rules or structured risk assessment processes.[web:1][web:12][web:9]

AI adoption patterns differ by size, digital maturity, geography and beneficiary focus. Large charities and infrastructure bodies are more advanced, but early-stage and small charities are already using AI for grant fundraising and communications, often without formal governance or specialist safeguards.[web:1][web:12] Emerging initiatives from sector bodies and vendors are beginning to support responsible adoption, yet there remains limited evidence about AI use in front-line safeguarding, advice and high-risk beneficiary services.

## Scope

This pack synthesises current evidence on how UK charities are adopting AI in 2025026, with a focus on:

- Overall adoption rates and stages of AI maturity.
- Differences in AI use by charity size, digital maturity and area of work.
- Commonly used AI products and patterns of use (including workplace tools and embedded features).
- Staff and trustee confidence, AI literacy, skills gaps and training initiatives.
- Governance maturity, policy availability and emerging approaches to risk management.
- Shadow AI, unapproved staff use and informal experimentation.
- Cross-cutting concerns: data protection, safeguarding, cybersecurity, bias, accessibility and public trust.
- Cost, capacity and procurement barriers.
- Current examples of charity AI policies and sector initiatives that support safe use.

The pack focuses on UK charities and wider voluntary organisations, drawing selectively on transferable evidence from public services, healthcare and other sectors where governance, privacy and safeguarding issues are comparable.[web:1][web:2][web:5] It does not attempt to catalogue every AI tool available or to provide detailed technical specifications; those will be covered in later tool-comparison packs.

## Research questions

1. How widely are UK charities using AI in 2025026, and how has adoption changed over time?
2. How does AI use vary by charity size, digital maturity, geography and area of charitable work?
3. Which categories of AI use are most common (e.g. drafting content, fundraising, grant prospecting, internal administration, service delivery)?
4. How confident are staff and trustees about AI, and where are the main skills gaps and literacy issues?
5. How mature is AI governance across the sector, including strategies, policies, risk registers and trustee oversight?
6. How prevalent are shadow AI and unapproved experimentation using consumer accounts?
7. What practical concerns do charities report about data protection, safeguarding, cybersecurity, bias, accessibility and public trust?
8. What cost, capacity and procurement barriers affect AI adoption, particularly for micro and small charities?
9. What examples exist of charity AI policies or governance approaches, and which sector initiatives are helping charities adopt AI responsibly?
10. Where is the evidence base strong, limited or biased towards particular segments of the sector?

## Method

### Source identification

Evidence was identified through targeted web searches for UK charity AI adoption surveys, sector reports, and governance resources published from 2024 to mid-2026.[web:1][web:2][web:3][web:6][web:9][web:10][web:11] Priority was given to:

- Large-scale UK charity surveys such as the Charity Digital Skills Report 2025.[web:1][web:12]
- The Phoenix Software/NCVO "State of AI in Charities 2025" survey.[web:9][web:2]
- Charity-sector analyses and commentaries in Civil Society, NCVO, Media Trust and other infrastructure bodies.[web:3][web:5][web:15]
- Fundraising-specific AI adoption data from the Status of UK Fundraising 2025 report.[web:7]
- Credible professional analysis and sector round-ups that synthesise multiple primary reports.[web:8][web:11][web:10]

Where sector publications referenced regulator guidance or legislation (e.g. UK GDPR), the legal and regulatory sources themselves are catalogued in Pack 12 and cross-referenced in thematic packs.[web:1][web:12]

### Period and jurisdictions

The research prioritises sources from January 2024 to July 2026, with emphasis on reports published in 2025026.[web:1][web:6][web:9][web:10] Jurisdictional focus is the United Kingdom, with attention to nation-specific findings (England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland) where surveys disaggregate results.[web:1] EU-wide or global AI data is used only to contextualise UK findings where clearly comparable and not presented as UK-specific practice.

### Evidence hierarchy and limitations

Sources were assessed using an adapted evidence hierarchy:

1. UK legislation and statutory regulators (for cross-pack reference rather than adoption statistics).
2. Official charity-sector surveys and infrastructure-body reports (e.g. Charity Digital Skills Report, NCVO partnerships).[web:1][web:9]
3. Established charity-sector research and fundraising studies.[web:1][web:7]
4. Vendor-sponsored sector surveys with transparent methodology (Phoenix/NCVO).[web:2][web:9]
5. Credible professional analysis and sector commentary (Civil Society, ICAEW, narrative blogs).[web:3][web:6][web:8][web:11]
6. Organisational case studies (e.g. NCVO Almanac AI use, Media Trust AI bootcamps).[web:9][web:15]

Key limitations:

- Several adoption statistics come from self-reported surveys, which may over-represent digitally engaged organisations and under-represent micro, volunteer-led and very small charities without capacity to respond.[web:1][web:9]
- Some surveys are vendor-sponsored, introducing potential bias towards highlighting opportunities and readiness for consultancy or product adoption; findings are used with caution and clearly labelled.[web:2][web:9]
- Evidence is strongest for general AI adoption, drafting and fundraising; robust data is limited for AI use in high-risk safeguarding or front-line beneficiary contexts.
- There is limited longitudinal data specifically distinguishing embedded AI features in existing systems from explicit AI tools.

## Key findings

1. **AI adoption is now mainstream but uneven.** Around three-quarters of UK charities report using AI in some way, but most are at an exploratory stage and few use AI strategically across their organisation.[web:1][web:12][web:6][web:9]
2. **Large and digitally advanced charities are more likely to use AI and to be developing policies and strategies, but early-stage and small charities are already experimenting heavily in fundraising and communications.**[web:1][web:12]
3. **Common use cases cluster around low-risk internal tasks and income generation:** drafting documents and reports, administrative tasks, online content and fundraising/grant-writing, with relatively little current use in service delivery, impact evaluation or automated decision-making.[web:12][web:7][web:9]
4. **Skills and literacy gaps are significant across the sector, particularly among boards and CEOs, with many charities rating their AI skills as poor and expressing low confidence in lawful, ethical and responsible use.**[web:1][web:12][web:2][web:9]
5. **Governance maturity lags behind adoption:** only a minority of charities report having AI policies, acceptable-use rules, AI strategies or structured AI risk registers, and almost none report comprehensive AI strategies.[web:1][web:12][web:2][web:9]
6. **Shadow AI and informal experimentation are widespread, with many staff using consumer tools or individual accounts without organisation-wide governance, particularly in smaller charities.**[web:2][web:9]
7. **Data protection, privacy, security, factual accuracy, bias and discrimination are the most commonly cited barriers to further AI adoption, alongside lack of skills, training and time.**[web:1][web:12][web:2][web:9]
8. **Cost and capacity remain structural constraints:** squeezed organisational finances, limited core funding for digital, and lack of headspace are major barriers to both general digital progress and structured AI adoption.[web:1][web:5]
9. **Public-trust and beneficiary-trust dimensions are recognised but not yet widely evidenced through direct beneficiary research; only a small minority of charities engage clients or communities about AI use.**[web:12][web:15]
10. **Sector initiatives and partnerships (e.g. Media Trust/NCVO AI bootcamps, Phoenix/NCVO governance workshops) are beginning to build confidence, but reach is limited relative to sector size.**[web:9][web:15]

## Detailed research

### Overall AI adoption rates and trends

The Charity Digital Skills Report 2025, based on responses from 672 UK charities and wider non-profit organisations, describes AI as moving from experimentation to mainstream use across the sector.[web:1][web:12] It reports that **76% of charities are using AI**, up from **61% in 2024**, with respondents categorised by stage of AI maturity:

- 2% using AI strategically or planning to scale AI across the organisation.
- 23% piloting and using AI actively.
- 51% exploring or informally testing AI tools.

Large charities show higher adoption: **89% of large charities** use AI compared with **72% of small charities**.[web:12] Charities with a digital strategy (advanced or advancing stages) report particularly high AI use, with **91%** using AI tools.[web:1][web:12]

A smaller Phoenix/NCVO survey of 39 charities, summarised in "The State of AI in Charities 2025", finds that **56%** of surveyed charities use AI in day-to-day operations, with another **23%** planning to adopt it, suggesting a similar pattern of majority but not universal adoption.[web:2][web:9] In this sample, 56% are exploring how AI could support the organisation, 36% are evaluating specific capabilities, and 41% are building AI policies or strategies, reinforcing the picture of widespread interest but early-stage implementation.[web:2][web:9]

Fundraising-specific data from the 2025 Status of UK Fundraising report indicates that AI usage in UK fundraising has risen from **57% in 2024 to 77% in 2025**, with most nonprofits using generative AI for content creation (newsletters, social media, reports) and a smaller subset using predictive AI for donor behaviour forecasting or chatbots.[web:7]

Qualitative sector commentary from Civil Society and ICAEW emphasises that AI adoption "shows no sign of slowing down" and is now a significant element of wider digital transformation debates, but also highlights persistent gaps in strategy, data and skills.[web:3][web:6][web:8]

Overall, these sources suggest that AI adoption rates are high and rising, but heavily skewed towards experimental and tactical use rather than integrated organisational systems.

### Adoption by charity size and digital maturity

The Charity Digital Skills Report differentiates AI adoption by organisation size and digital maturity, offering a granular view of who is using AI and how.[web:1][web:12]

- **Large charities**: 89% report using AI, and 68% are developing an AI policy.[web:12] They are more likely to use AI for administrative tasks (64%), attend AI-related events and training, and create internal AI working groups.[web:12]
- **Small charities**: 72% report using AI, with 37% developing an AI policy.[web:12] Small charities are disproportionately using AI for grant fundraising (43%) and communications/fundraising (38%), often from an early digital starting point.[web:12]
- **Early-stage ("curious") charities**: Surprisingly, 62% of charities at the earliest stage of digital maturity (limited skills and capacity) report using AI tools; however, most are exploring (48%) rather than implementing AI strategically (13%).[web:12]

This pattern indicates that AI experimentation is not limited to digitally advanced or large organisations. Many early-stage and smaller charities are attracted by low-barrier consumer tools and embedded features, particularly for grant fundraising and communications, even when broader digital strategy and governance are underdeveloped.[web:1][web:12]

Regional data in the Charity Digital Skills Report shows that AI uptake is slightly lower in the Midlands and Northern Ireland (67% reporting AI use) and higher in Scotland (where AI skills for everyday use are rated more positively).[web:1] These differences correlate with wider digital maturity and funding access, suggesting that geography and local infrastructure support also influence AI adoption.[web:1]

Vendor-sponsored research from Phoenix/NCVO emphasises that skills, resources and risk management are particular challenges for smaller organisations with legacy systems and limited staff capacity, reinforcing the need for proportionate governance support.[web:2][web:9]

### Commonly used AI products and categories of use

Current evidence does not provide comprehensive product-level adoption statistics; most surveys focus on categories of use rather than specific tools.[web:1][web:12][web:2] However, several patterns emerge:

- **Day-to-day generative AI use:** The Charity Digital Skills Report notes that half of charities (50%) use AI for documents and reports, including drafting and structuring content; 36% use AI for developing online content; and 35% for idea generation.[web:12]
- **Administrative tasks:** 48% use AI for administrative tasks, up from 32% in 2024; large charities report higher use (64%) than small charities (43%).[web:12]
- **Research and information gathering:** 29% use AI for research and information gathering, up from 24%.[web:12]
- **Data analysis:** AI use for data analysis is rare: 15% for qualitative analysis, 13% for numerical data and 4% for predictive analytics.[web:12]

At organisational level, the top reported uses are:[web:12]

1. Administration and project management (48%).
2. Grant fundraising (36%, rising to 43% among small charities).
3. Communications and fundraising (34%, almost double the 19% reported in 2024).

Relatively few charities report using AI for impact and evaluation (17%), service delivery (7%, down from 12% in 2024), strategic planning (9%) or recruitment and HR (5%).[web:12] This suggests that AI is currently concentrated in back-office and income-generation functions rather than in core beneficiary services or high-risk decision-making.

Fundraising-focused research corroborates that generative AI is primarily being used for content creation, with smaller groups exploring predictive analytics and chatbot applications.[web:7]

Sector commentaries (e.g. Civil Society, CharityIQ) highlight that charities are experimenting with widely-available tools such as consumer ChatGPT accounts, workplace products (e.g. Microsoft Copilot) and embedded AI features in CRMs, but detailed product-level adoption data remains sparse and will be addressed in Pack 06.[web:3][web:9][web:10]

### Differences by area of charitable work and beneficiary focus

Most adoption surveys are not stratified by detailed sub-sector (e.g. childrens services, health charities, advice organisations), but some insights can be inferred.

- The Charity Digital Skills Report notes particularly strong digital inclusion support among black-led charities, disabled or d/Deaf-led organisations and charities in specific regions, which may influence how these organisations approach AI in service delivery.[web:1]
- Phoenix/NCVOs report emphasises that charities handling sensitive beneficiary information face greater pressure to address privacy, data protection and ethical concerns before expanding AI use.[web:2][web:9]
- Media Trust/NCVO AI bootcamps involved a wide mix of charities, including advice and health-related organisations, but the documented examples focus mainly on communications, fundraising and volunteer management, not direct clinical or safeguarding AI use.[web:15]

Overall, there is currently limited, publicly available evidence on AI adoption stratified by beneficiary group (e.g. children, adults at risk), sector (e.g. health vs arts) or service type. This is a critical evidence gap for high-risk uses and will be revisited in Packs 05, 08 and 11.

### Staff confidence, trustee confidence and AI literacy

Multiple sources highlight low AI confidence and limited literacy across the sector.

- The Charity Digital Skills Report finds that 35% of charities say they are poor at using AI tools in everyday work and 29% do not use them, meaning 64% either struggle or are not using AI tools in daily work.[web:1][web:12]
- Half of charities (50%) report difficulty keeping up with emerging tech trends; 41% say they are poor at this and 9% do not track trends.[web:12]
- 36% of charities say their CEO has poor AI skills, knowledge and confidence; only 8% see their CEO as excellent at AI.[web:12]
- 44% say their boards are poor at AI; only 3% rate their board as excellent.[web:12]

Phoenix/NCVOs survey paints a similar picture:

- 95% of organisations believe they have low knowledge around AI.[web:2]
- 82% report low confidence in understanding how to use AI lawfully, ethically and responsibly.[web:2]
- 68% have low confidence that they understand AI risks; 74% lack confidence that they have meaningful human control over AI systems.[web:2]
- 82% report low confidence in their organisations AI expertise.[web:2]

Media Trusts AI Essentials Bootcamps report that only 31% of participating staff felt confident using AI for everyday tasks before the programme, with confidence rising to 85% afterwards, illustrating both the baseline gap and the potential impact of targeted training.[web:15]

Taken together, these findings indicate that while experimentation with AI is widespread, staff and trustee confidence, AI literacy and structured training remain major challenges. Many charities rely on informal experimentation, peer learning and sector events rather than systematic AI skills development.[web:12][web:15]

### Governance maturity, policies and strategies

Evidence consistently shows that AI governance is less developed than AI use.

In the Charity Digital Skills Report:

- 48% of charities are developing an AI policy, up from 16% in 2024, with 68% of large charities and 37% of small charities doing so.[web:1][web:12]
- 39% ensure AI aligns with organisational values and policies; 36% review data protection and security; 36% avoid AI in potentially harmful areas.[web:12]
- Only 23% update risk registers to reflect AI; 22% review governance to give trustees better oversight of AI; and 20% undertake regular assessments of AI risks such as bias, privacy and misinformation; the report explicitly notes this as a concern.[web:12]
- 37% of charities report not having undertaken any actions to move forward with AI, indicating that many remain in ad hoc experimentation without strategic analysis or governance.[web:12]

Phoenix/NCVOs report finds similarly limited governance structures:

- Only 10% of respondents have an AI policy; 41% have one in progress; 41% have no policy.[web:2]
- Only 7% have an AI Acceptable Use Policy (AUP); 23% have one in progress; 62% do not have one.[web:2]
- 0% have a comprehensive AI strategy in place; 41% have a strategy in progress; 60% have no strategy or plans.[web:2]
- 70% lack AI literacy training; 67% lack security awareness training that addresses AI risks.[web:2]

These findings suggest that while AI policies are emergingparticularly among larger charitiescomprehensive AI strategies, acceptable-use frameworks and integrated governance models are rare. Boards often lack digital and AI skills, and few organisations have defined structured oversight roles for trustees or senior leaders.

### Shadow AI and unapproved staff use

Evidence of shadow AIAI used outside organisational governanceis mainly qualitative but consistent.

- Phoenix/NCVO notes that staff often use personal ChatGPT accounts daily even where organisational AI use is not formally approved, with quotes such as "Im using AI daily via my own ChatGPT account, but we arent using it across the organisation yet".[web:2]
- The report also highlights that 44% of respondents see slow user adoption of formal AI solutions, and that AI often "happens at the edges" rather than being centrally managed.[web:2]
- Charity Digital Skills Report findings that 59% of charities learn about AI by experimenting with tools in their job, and that 33% do not know how to get started with AI, imply substantial informal use without structured approval or training.[web:12]

Overall, the evidence confirms that shadow AI is common, particularly in smaller organisations, and that informal experimentation often precedes or bypasses organisational AI approvals or risk assessments.

### Data protection, safeguarding, security, accuracy and bias concerns

Data protection and privacy concerns are among the most frequently cited barriers to AI adoption.

- Charity Digital Skills Report: 43% of charities cite data privacy, GDPR and security concerns as a barrier to AI progress, up from 31% in 2024; this rises to 56% of large charities.[web:1][web:12]
- 32% highlight potential bias and discrimination as a barrier; for large charities this is the second biggest barrier (46%).[web:12]
- 33% are worried about factual accuracy, rising to 42% for large charities.[web:12]
- 26% report concerns about environmental impact, and 25% report that data maturity must be improved before expanding AI use.[web:12]

Phoenix/NCVO reports similar themes:

- 77% of respondents worry about their ability to handle sensitive data and prevent privacy risks.[web:2]
- 80% highlight lack of skills and expertise as undermining secure and effective AI implementation.[web:2]

These findings sit against a broader backdrop in which many charities report that digital policies and governance, and strengthening data protection and cybersecurity, are a low priority (23% describing these as low priority in the Charity Digital Skills Report), despite AI adoption increasing.[web:1]

While specific safeguarding and beneficiary safety concerns are not deeply analysed in these surveys, the consistent emphasis on privacy, bias, discrimination and misinformation creates a strong rationale for proportionate controls in contexts where AI touches beneficiary data or advice. Detailed safeguarding analysis is addressed in Pack 05.

### Accessibility, inclusion and public trust

The Charity Digital Skills Report provides relevant context on accessibility and inclusion in digital services.

- Only 28% of charities see accessibility, diversity and inclusion in digital services as a top priority, although this figure is higher among marginalised-group-led charities (4053%).[web:1]
- Only 15% say their services are informed to a great extent by research with users from diverse communities, and 72% report some level of co-design.[web:1]

Media Trusts AI bootcamp evaluation emphasises that charities want help to engage with AI safely, ethically and effectively, and describes participant concerns around being "left behind" by AI developments.[web:15] The same article notes that few charities initially felt confident about whether they "should" be using AI, indicating a trust and legitimacy dimension in addition to technical risk.

There is limited direct evidence about beneficiary or public attitudes to charity AI use; only 6% of charities in the Charity Digital Skills Report report engaging clients, users or communities about their AI use, and only 16% report being transparent with external stakeholders about AI use.[web:12] This is a notable evidence gap, with implications for trust and accountability.

### Cost, capacity and procurement barriers

AI adoption sits within broader digital funding and capacity constraints.

- 69% of charities identify squeezed organisational finances as the biggest barrier to digital progress; 64% cite lack of funds to invest in infrastructure, systems and tools; and 63% highlight lack of headspace and capacity.[web:1]
- 60% of charities have not accessed funding for digital costs in the past 12 months; 37% say time is the biggest barrier to applying for digital funding, with this particularly acute for small and early-stage charities.[web:1]
- Finding a funder who supports digital projects is the biggest barrier for 27% of charities; this rises to 44% of black-led charities and around one-third of lived-experience-led organisations.[web:1]

Phoenix/NCVO notes that cost, time and resource constraints, especially in smaller organisations with legacy systems, are major obstacles to implementing AI; even when solutions are deployed, patchy adoption and difficulty demonstrating return on investment can fuel leadership hesitation.[web:2][web:9]

These structural constraints help explain why many charities remain at the exploratory stage of AI adoption and why governance investment may lag behind informal experimentation.

### Current examples of charity AI policies and sector initiatives

Evidence of published charity AI policies remains limited; most sources describe policy development in general terms rather than providing detailed templates.

- Charity Digital Skills Report: 48% of charities are developing an AI policy, tripling from 16% in 2024; large charities are more likely than small to be doing so.[web:1][web:12]
- Phoenix/NCVO: 10% of surveyed organisations have an AI policy; 41% have one in progress; 41% have none.[web:2]

Sector initiatives provide some emerging models:

- Phoenix/NCVO promote AI Governance Workshops and bespoke AI governance support, referencing alignment with frameworks such as the ICOs Data Protection Toolkit, NIST AI Risk Management Framework, ISO 42001:2023 and the UK Governments AI Playbook.[web:2]
- NCVOs Civil Society Almanac 2025 pilot integrates AI tools to process large volumes of charity financial accounts, with explicit emphasis on ethical and efficient use; this is a documented case of AI in research rather than front-line services.[web:2][web:9]
- Media Trust/NCVO AI Essentials Bootcamps delivered structured training on practical use of AI, ethics and strategic grounding, reaching 125 charities and significantly increasing confidence in AI use.[web:15]

These initiatives demonstrate practical steps towards responsible AI adoption but currently reach a small proportion of the overall sector, reinforcing the need for scalable, proportionate governance models.

## Implications by charity size

### Micro and volunteer-led charities

- Likely to experiment with free or low-cost consumer AI tools for drafting emails, social media posts and small grant applications, often without formal governance or policies.[web:12][web:2]
- High risk of shadow AI, particularly where volunteers use personal accounts and devices; data-protection and safeguarding risks arise if personal or sensitive information is entered into general-purpose tools.[web:2][web:9]
- Capacity constraints make complex governance frameworks unrealistic; minimum viable controls include a simple policy, named responsible person, clear rules on what data must never be entered into AI tools, and basic training on privacy and accuracy.

### Small charities

- Evidence shows strong use of AI for grant fundraising and communications, even from early digital maturity stages.[web:12][web:7]
- Barriers centre on limited digital skills, lack of training, data privacy concerns and lack of time to seek funding for digital/AI.[web:1][web:12]
- Proportionate governance may include a concise AI acceptable-use policy, simple risk screening for new tools, clear limits on beneficiary data use, and lightweight trustee oversight integrated into existing risk registers.

### Medium-sized charities

- Often have more structured digital strategies and access to infrastructure support; likely to pilot workplace AI products (e.g. Microsoft Copilot, CRM-embedded AI) and explore AI in impact measurement and reporting.[web:1][web:9]
- Governance maturity varies; some develop AI policies and strategies, but many still lack comprehensive oversight and training.
- Proportionate controls may include formal AI tool approval processes, DPIA screening for higher-risk use cases, explicit connector and data-access controls, and regular reporting to trustees on AI-related risks.

### Large and complex charities

- Highest adoption rates and most active in developing AI policies, strategies and internal governance structures.[web:1][web:12][web:2]
- Also report the greatest concerns about data privacy, bias, discrimination, accuracy and environmental impacts, reflecting both more advanced use and exposure to complex risk scenarios.[web:12][web:2]
- Expected to implement more formal elements: AI strategy aligned with digital and data strategies; board-level oversight; comprehensive AI acceptable-use policies; structured training; risk registers that explicitly cover AI; DPIA processes for high-risk systems; and strong supplier due diligence.

## Practical implications

- AI is already part of everyday charity work, especially for drafting and administration. Governance responses must therefore address current practice, not only future systems.
- Many charities are using AI without clear rules on what information may be entered; immediate priority is establishing simple, proportionate policies on prohibited data types (e.g. identifiable beneficiary data, special-category data, sensitive case notes) for general-purpose AI tools.
- The gap between AI adoption and governance maturity suggests that trustee and leadership oversight needs to focus on identifying where AI is already in use, rather than only approving new systems.
- Given widespread skills gaps and low confidence, practical guidance, training and peer support are critical; sector initiatives demonstrate that confidence can be significantly improved with accessible training.
- Differences by size and capacity mean that governance tools (policies, registers, checklists) must be scalable from micro to large charities, with clear minimum practice and stronger practice expectations.

## Recommended controls

The following high-level controls respond to the adoption and governance patterns evidenced above. Detailed templates are developed in Pack 09.

### People and accountability

- Designate an AI lead or responsible person (could be part of an existing role) to coordinate AI governance and act as a point of contact.
- Ensure trustees and senior leaders receive at least an introductory briefing on AI adoption, risks and the organisations policy position.
- Provide accessible training for staff and volunteers on safe, proportionate AI use, focusing on what information may be entered, accuracy checks and bias awareness.[web:12][web:2][web:15]

### Policies and processes

- Develop an AI acceptable-use policy or integrate AI rules into existing digital and data policies, covering approved tools, prohibited uses and documentation requirements.[web:2][web:12]
- Implement a simple AI tool approval process, requiring basic assessment of data protection, safeguarding and security implications before adoption.
- Record AI use cases and tools in an AI use register, even in micro organisations, to support transparency and review.

### Data protection

- Define which data categories must not be entered into general-purpose AI tools (e.g. identifiable beneficiary data, health information, safeguarding concerns, case notes, childrens data) and when anonymisation or synthetic data are required.
- Treat AI-related processing as part of wider data-protection compliance under UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018, including lawful basis, transparency and individual rights; use DPIA screening for higher-risk uses.[web:1][web:12][web:2]

### Safeguarding

- Require specialist safeguarding review for any AI use that touches beneficiary casework, advice, triage or monitoring; avoid deployment where human support cannot remain immediately available.
- Explicitly limit automation in safeguarding contexts and ensure escalation pathways to trained people.

### Procurement and contracts

- For workplace AI products and connectors, review vendor documentation on data retention, training on customer data, security controls, data residency and subprocessors; ensure appropriate data-processing agreements are in place.
- Avoid relying solely on vendor marketing claims about safety or privacy; seek evidence in contracts and technical documentation.[web:2]

### Technical security

- Prefer organisational accounts with identity management (SSO, role-based access) over unmanaged individual consumer accounts.
- Configure connector permissions carefully for systems such as Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace, ensuring permission inheritance does not expose sensitive shared-drive or CRM data.

### Human review

- Require meaningful human review for AI-generated outputs used in communications, fundraising, grant applications and internal decision-making, including fact checking and bias checks.
- Make clear that AI outputs are suggestions, not authoritative advice, and that staff remain accountable for decisions.

### Monitoring and assurance

- Integrate AI-related risks into existing risk registers and assurance processes, with simple metrics such as number of approved AI tools, training coverage and incidents/near misses.
- For larger charities, consider periodic internal audits of AI use, including shadow AI and connector configurations.

### Incident response

- Include AI-related incidents (e.g. data leakage via AI tools, harmful AI-generated advice, biased profiling) in incident reporting and review processes.
- Assess whether serious AI-related incidents may be relevant to regulator reporting thresholds (e.g. Charity Commission serious incident reports, ICO breach notifications) on a case-by-case basis.

## Proportionate implementation

### Minimum practice

- A basic AI policy or acceptable-use statement.
- Clear rules on prohibited data types and sensitive contexts.
- A simple AI use register or log.
- Named responsible person for AI governance.
- Basic staff and volunteer training on safe use.

### Stronger practice

- AI tool approval forms with risk screening.
- Trustee oversight integrated into governance structures.
- DPIA screening for higher-risk use cases.
- Structured supplier questionnaires and contract reviews.
- More extensive training for staff in roles heavily using AI.

### High-risk or complex-use requirements

- Full DPIAs for AI systems influencing decisions about people or processing special-category data, particularly in safeguarding, health or advice contexts.
- Board-level approval for public-facing AI systems, automated decision-making and AI connectors to core systems (e.g. CRM, case-management platforms).
- Ongoing monitoring and testing of AI behaviour, bias and accuracy, with clear escalation routes.

## Questions charities should ask

### Staff and volunteers

- What AI tools am I using, and are they approved by the organisation?
- What types of information am I entering into AI tools, and does this comply with our policy?
- How am I checking the accuracy, bias and appropriateness of AI outputs before using them?

### Managers and senior leaders

- Where is AI already in use across our organisation, including shadow AI?
- Do we have an AI policy and acceptable-use guidance, and is it proportionate to our size and risks?
- Are our risk registers, data-protection documentation and safeguarding procedures updated to reflect AI use?

### Trustees

- What AI systems and tools are the charity using, and for what purposes?
- How have risks around data protection, safeguarding, bias and public trust been assessed and managed?
- What training and support do staff and volunteers receive, and how is governance reviewed over time?

### Data-protection leads

- Which AI uses may require DPIA screening or a full DPIA?
- How are lawful basis, transparency and individual rights being addressed for AI-related processing?
- What information must never be entered into general-purpose AI tools, and how is this communicated?

### Safeguarding leads

- Are any AI tools interacting directly with beneficiaries, or processing safeguarding-related information?
- What escalation pathways exist when AI is involved in advice, triage or monitoring?
- How are safeguarding risks from automation, hallucinations and bias being monitored?

### HR and recruitment

- Are we considering AI in recruitment or shortlisting, and if so, how are fairness, discrimination and transparency risks managed?
- Are staff and volunteers supported to use AI safely in their roles, and how is training recorded?

### Fundraising and communications

- How are AI tools used to draft fundraising and campaign content, and what human-review processes apply?
- Are we profiling or segmenting donors using AI, and how are data-protection and fairness risks addressed?

### Technology and procurement teams

- What workplace AI products are in use or under consideration, and what do vendor documentation and contracts say about data use, retention and training?
- How are connectors and access controls configured to prevent oversharing of sensitive internal data?

## Evidence gaps and uncertainties

- Limited charity-size-specific adoption data beyond broad categories; micro and volunteer-led charities remain under-represented in surveys.
- Limited evidence about beneficiaries views on charity AI use, and about public trust implications.
- Limited documentation of AI-related incidents or near misses specifically within the UK charity sector.
- Sparse evidence on AI use in high-risk safeguarding, crisis support and mental-health services.
- Limited independent testing of workplace AI controls (e.g. retention settings, connector behaviour) in charity contexts.
- Limited data on long-term organisational effects of AI adoption, including staff workload, burnout and changes in governance culture.
- Very limited evidence about volunteer use of AI and shadow AI by non-staff contributors.

Where evidence cannot be confirmed or is based mainly on vendor-sponsored research, findings in this pack are described cautiously and flagged as limited or indicative rather than definitive.

## Conclusions

The available evidence shows that AI adoption among UK charities has moved rapidly from niche experimentation to mainstream use between 2024 and 2025026, with around three-quarters of charities reporting some use of AI tools and widespread interest in practical applications for administration, fundraising and content creation.[web:1][web:12][web:7][web:9] However, adoption is uneven by size, digital maturity and geography, and governance, skills and confidence lag significantly behind the pace of experimentation.

Large, digitally advanced and infrastructure organisations are leading in developing AI policies, strategies and governance workshops, but early-stage and small charities are already using AI heavily in grant fundraising and communications, often without structured safeguards.[web:1][web:12][web:2][web:9] Sector-wide surveys reveal consistent concerns about data protection, privacy, bias, discrimination and factual accuracy, alongside limited AI literacy and low confidence in lawful and ethical use.

For the AI Safe Use Playbook, these findings underline the need for proportionate, evidence-led governance models that can support charities of all sizes. Minimum practice must address shadow AI and everyday generative use, while stronger and high-risk practice models are required for complex, sensitive and public-facing systems. Subsequent packs will build on this adoption evidence to define practical use cases, risk classifications, safeguarding requirements, governance templates and supplier comparisons.
