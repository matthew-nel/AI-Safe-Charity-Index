# Show the 20

Key findings yes

Here are 20 cross-pack key findings that emerge from the evidence base, sized for UK charities of all kinds.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

## Adoption and governance

1. AI use is now mainstream across UK charities, with sector surveys indicating around three-quarters or more of organisations using AI tools in some form by 2025–2026, often for drafting bids, communications and basic analysis.[^5][^6][^7][^3]
2. Governance has not kept pace: multiple reports find a “significant and worsening gap” between widespread AI use and limited board-level oversight, with most charities lacking a robust AI strategy, policy or register.[^2][^8][^9][^1][^5]
3. Trustees are often unaware of AI use: Charity Commission survey data suggests only a small minority of trustees report their charity using AI, despite staff and volunteers quietly using tools day to day.[^10][^1]
4. Shadow AI and consumer accounts are a major risk route, with staff and volunteers commonly using free tools (e.g. consumer ChatGPT and Claude) for charity work without central control or training.[^8][^3][^11][^5]

## Public trust and beneficiary attitudes

5. Public attitudes towards AI in charities are cautious and conditional: around a third of UK adults support charity AI use, a similar share oppose it, and many are unsure.[^12][^13][^4]
6. The public strongly prefers AI to support back-office efficiency and fraud detection, but trust drops sharply when AI influences life-changing decisions about who receives support or handles sensitive personal data.[^13][^4]
7. Beneficiary-specific and vulnerability-focused research (especially in mental health) shows that general-purpose chatbots can provide inaccurate, unsafe advice, and that harms disproportionately affect already marginalised groups.[^14][^15][^16][^17][^18]
8. Synthetic images and AI-generated beneficiary stories create reputational risks: emerging charity-specific studies warn that “AI shortcuts to empathy” may undermine authenticity and trust if not used transparently and carefully.[^19][^20][^13]

## Risk patterns and incidents

9. Hallucination and misinformation are consistent LLM failure modes: case reports and studies show chatbots fabricating citations, repeating false medical information, and producing dangerously incorrect advice when prompts seed errors.[^16][^17][^21][^22]
10. Documented severe harms in mental-health contexts have primarily involved unintended use of general companion chatbots rather than clinically governed tools, underlining the danger of relying on general-purpose AI for support.[^14][^16]
11. Bias and discrimination risks are well evidenced in public-sector algorithmic decision-making: UK and international reviews show AI systems can replicate and amplify structural inequalities in welfare, policing and other services, with clear lessons for charity profiling and triage.[^23][^24][^25][^26][^27]
12. Charity-specific incident data remains sparse, but sector and regulator commentary emphasise that any AI-related data breach, unsafe advice or discriminatory impact could have serious safeguarding and reputational consequences.[^15][^28][^29]

## Regulation and governance expectations

13. UK GDPR, Data Protection Act 2018 and PECR apply fully to AI-enabled charity activities; ICO’s AI guidance and risk toolkit clarify fairness, transparency and automated decision-making obligations for organisations deploying AI.[^30][^31][^32]
14. Charity Commission and governance bodies expect boards to treat AI as part of core trustee duties—integrating AI into existing risk management, safeguarding and data-protection frameworks, not as a separate technical issue.[^33][^28][^34][^35]
15. The Charity Governance Code refresh and sector guidance explicitly reference technology and AI policy as evidence of good governance, with recommendations for AI strategies, named responsibility and board-level training.[^34][^36][^1]

## Workplace tools and connectors

16. Major workplace AI products (Microsoft 365 Copilot, Google Workspace with Gemini, ChatGPT Enterprise/Business, Claude Team/Enterprise) offer enterprise controls—no training on customer data by default, DPAs, SSO, audit logs—but effective safety depends heavily on underlying identity, permissions and retention governance.[^37][^38][^39][^40][^41]
17. Connectors to internal systems (SharePoint, OneDrive, Google Drive, Slack, CRMs) are a critical risk point: evidence shows oversharing typically stems from misconfigured source permissions rather than the AI layer itself, meaning charities must harden their data foundations before enabling AI search and summarisation.[^39][^42][^43][^37]

## Safeguarding and proportionate use

18. Safeguarding and mental-health guidance consistently advise against using general-purpose AI for crisis support, clinical advice or beneficiary triage; AI may have safer roles in administrative support (e.g. summarising anonymised feedback, translating non-sensitive materials) under clear boundaries and human oversight.[^44][^45][^15][^16][^14]
19. Sector evidence supports a Green/Amber/Red approach: low-risk “Green” uses centre on public, anonymised or internal non-sensitive content with human review; “Amber” uses (profiling, segmentation, chatbots, connectors) require DPIA screening, controls and specialist input; “Red” uses (AI deciding who gets support, interacting directly with vulnerable beneficiaries, handling case notes in consumer tools) should generally be redesigned or avoided.[^46][^4][^47][^48][^23]

## Capacity, effectiveness and priorities

20. AI is delivering real but uneven benefits: sector studies report time savings and improved fundraising or bid-writing in some charities, while others see limited impact; the most pressing priority is not more AI adoption but closing the governance gap—policies, registers, DPIAs, training and trustee oversight—to use existing tools safely and proportionately.[^7][^49][^5][^2][^8]
