# AI in Charity Fundraising

## Executive summary

Recent UK fundraising research shows rapid growth in AI use: the Status of UK Fundraising 2025 report finds that AI use among UK not-for-profits rose from 57% in 2024 to 77% in 2025, with most respondents using free-to-access generative AI tools to draft fundraising and communications content.[web:65][web:70][web:11] At the same time, only a small minority of organisations report having formal AI policies or governance frameworks for fundraising, and many fundraisers express concerns about ethics, data privacy, misinformation, and regulatory compliance.[web:11][web:72][web:73]

In December 2025, the Fundraising Regulator published guidance on using AI in fundraising and an AI policy for its own operations, emphasising that charities remain accountable for AI-assisted fundraising, should conduct proportionate risk assessments, adopt AI policies, ensure data protection and cybersecurity, and maintain meaningful human oversight of AI-generated content and decisions.[web:62][web:58][web:66] UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018, PECR and the Code of Fundraising Practice together shape a robust compliance environment for AI-enabled fundraising, particularly where personal data, profiling, automated decision-making or vulnerable donors are involved.[web:62][web:58][web:65][web:70]

This pack synthesises evidence on AI use in UK charity fundraising, including drafting and editing appeals, personalised communications, donor profiling and segmentation, wealth screening, prospect research, predictive analytics, automated journeys, fundraising chatbots, synthetic media, grant prospecting, and supporter service automation. It distinguishes between AI drafting general communications, personalising messages, profiling individuals, predicting behaviour, supporting staff decisions and making or materially influencing decisions about people, and summarises Green/Amber/Red scenarios and proportionate controls.

## Scope

This pack covers AI use in fundraising by UK charities and wider non-profit organisations, specifically:

- Drafting and editing fundraising copy and appeals.
- Personalised appeals and tailored messaging.
- Donor profiling, segmentation and wealth screening.
- Prospect research and predictive analytics.
- Automated email journeys and supporter-service automation.
- Fundraising chatbots and virtual assistants.
- Synthetic campaign images and AI-generated beneficiary stories.
- Grant prospecting and applications.
- Legacy fundraising and major-donor research.

The analysis focuses on practical uses and their regulatory implications under UK GDPR, Data Protection Act 2018, PECR, direct marketing rules, consent, legitimate interests, special-category data, inferred information, profiling, automated decision-making, and the Fundraising Regulator’s guidance and Code of Fundraising Practice.[web:62][web:58][web:65][web:70] It does not offer legal advice, but aims to translate regulatory and sector guidance into proportionate practice for charities of different sizes.

## Research questions

1. How are UK charities using AI in fundraising as of 2025–2026, and how has usage grown over time?
2. Which AI fundraising activities are relatively low risk, and which require additional controls, approval or assessment?
3. How do UK GDPR, Data Protection Act 2018, PECR, direct marketing rules, consent, legitimate interests and special-category data requirements apply to AI-enabled fundraising?
4. How do profiling, inferred information and automated decision-making intersect with AI tools in fundraising, and what are the implications for fairness and transparency?
5. What does the Fundraising Regulator’s guidance on AI mean in practice for drafting content, personalisation, profiling and supporter service automation?
6. What Green/Amber/Red fundraising scenarios can charities use as practical reference points, and what controls are proportionate for different charity sizes?

### Period and jurisdiction

Evidence covers 2024–2026, with a focus on 2025–2026 sources that reflect current AI adoption in fundraising.[web:65][web:70][web:62][web:58] Jurisdiction is the United Kingdom; global or EU findings are referenced only for context and clearly distinguished.

### Evidence hierarchy and limitations

- Fundraising Regulator guidance and Code of Fundraising Practice interpret legal requirements and set sector standards for fundraising; they are treated as primary for fundraising-specific practice.[web:62][web:58]
- Blackbaud’s Status of UK Fundraising (and DSC commentary) provide quantitative data on AI use; as a vendor-sponsored survey, results are treated carefully and cross-referenced with independent commentary.[web:65][web:68][web:70]
- CIOF research offers qualitative insight into fundraisers’ concerns and readiness.[web:72]
- Legal articles and sector analyses provide interpretation of legal and regulatory frameworks, but are not independent regulators.[web:50][web:71]

Limitations:

- Survey data is self-reported and may over-represent digitally engaged fundraising teams.[web:65][web:70]
- AI incidents and harms in fundraising are not extensively documented; risk assessments draw on broader AI literature and regulator warnings about privacy, bias and misinformation.[web:62][web:58]

## Key findings

1. AI use in UK charity fundraising has grown rapidly, with around three-quarters of fundraising teams using AI tools for content creation, and a smaller proportion using AI for predictive analytics, donor profiling and chatbots.[web:65][web:70][web:11]
2. Most fundraising AI use currently focuses on drafting and editing copy, reports and social-media content, often using free or low-cost generative tools; far fewer organisations use AI for behaviour prediction or automated decision-making.[web:65][web:70][web:72]
3. Fundraisers perceive significant opportunities for efficiency and strategic insight, but express strong concerns about inaccurate outputs, misinformation, data security, bias, ethics and lack of governance.[web:70][web:72][web:73]
4. The Fundraising Regulator’s AI guidance emphasises accountability, proportionate risk assessments, AI policies, transparency, data protection and human oversight of AI-generated content and decisions.[web:62][web:58]
5. UK GDPR, PECR and direct marketing rules apply fully to AI-enabled fundraising, particularly where personal data is processed for profiling and targeted communications, requiring lawful basis, transparency and fairness.[web:62][web:58][web:65]
6. Vulnerable donors, legacy fundraising and major-donor research present heightened risks, requiring stricter safeguards, clear transparency and human judgement.[web:62][web:58][web:69]

## Detailed research

### AI adoption in fundraising: rates and patterns

The Status of UK Fundraising 2025 report, based on over 550 UK nonprofit professionals, finds that AI usage in fundraising has **surged from 57% in 2024 to 77% in 2025**, with most respondents feeling positively about AI’s impact.[web:65][web:70] The report shows that:

- **79%** of respondents using AI rely on free-to-access AI systems (e.g. consumer generative tools).[web:70]
- Only **26%** use paid standalone solutions; **18%** use AI embedded in CRM or donor databases; and **3%** have developed in-house AI tools.[web:70]
- **58%** use AI to draft content for newsletters, emails and reports; **44%** use AI for social-media drafts; **30%** use AI virtual assistants for tasks such as note-taking; **16%** use AI chatbots; and **11%** use AI to predict donor behaviour.[web:70]

DSC’s commentary underscores that AI is already seen as a key driver of efficiency, with many fundraisers expecting AI to help their organisations become more efficient (66% agreed, up from 45% the year before).[web:65][web:70] Yet only **16%** of organisations report having AI policies for fundraising, suggesting substantial governance gaps.[web:11][web:73]

CIOF’s survey, focusing on fundraisers’ perceptions, reports that only **4%** of respondents are “very familiar” with AI regulation policies, rising to just 9% among AI users, and that **90%** are unaware of any sector-specific guidelines to support fundraisers.[web:72] Efficiency is seen as the main opportunity, while concerns centre on ethics, data privacy and reliability.[web:72]

### Types of AI use in fundraising

Evidence indicates that fundraising AI use falls into several categories:

- **Drafting and editing copy:** Generative AI tools are used to draft appeals, newsletters, social media, reports and grant applications; this is the most common use.[web:65][web:70][web:11]
- **Personalisation:** Some organisations experiment with personalised subject lines, messaging and ask amounts based on segment-level data.[web:65][web:68]
- **Profiling and segmentation:** AI may be used to group donors based on giving history, engagement metrics and demographics, often through CRM-embedded features.[web:65][web:70]
- **Wealth screening and prospect research:** Tools such as AI-powered prospect assistants scan public sources for high-value donor leads, raising questions about fairness and transparency.[web:11]
- **Predictive analytics:** A smaller subset uses AI to forecast donor behaviour, campaign performance and propensity to give, often in larger, tech-savvy organisations.[web:65][web:70]
- **Automated journeys and chatbots:** Trials include AI-enabled email journeys, supporter FAQs and chatbots for online fundraising pages.[web:65][web:70]
- **Synthetic media and storytelling:** Some charities explore AI-generated images and narrative styles, raising ethical questions around authenticity and representation.[web:69]

### Regulatory framework: UK GDPR, PECR and direct marketing

AI-enabled fundraising sits within the existing regulatory environment for data protection and direct marketing:

- **UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018:** Personal data used for AI-driven profiling, segmentation or behavioural prediction must have a lawful basis (often legitimate interests), be fair, transparent and limited to what is necessary.[web:62][web:58] Individuals have rights to be informed about profiling, to object to direct marketing, and to challenge automated decisions.
- **PECR and direct marketing rules:** Email, SMS and other electronic fundraising communications must comply with PECR rules, including consent or soft opt-in requirements, depending on donor relationship and channel.[web:62][web:58]
- **Special-category data and inferred information:** Any AI use involving health, sexuality, religion or other special-category data (including inferred from behaviour or environment) requires particular care; lawful basis and conditions for processing must be carefully assessed.[web:62][web:58][web:69]

The Fundraising Regulator’s guidance on AI clarifies that using AI does not change the underlying obligations: charities must still comply with the Code of Fundraising Practice, including honesty, transparency, respect for donors and safeguards for vulnerable people.[web:62][web:58]

### Fundraising Regulator guidance on AI (December 2025)

The Fundraising Regulator’s guidance sets out key principles:

- **Accountability:** Charities remain responsible for all AI use in fundraising, including tools provided by third parties.[web:62][web:58]
- **Proportionate risk assessments:** Organisations are encouraged to conduct proportionate risk assessments before using AI tools, to identify potential errors, bias and impacts.[web:58][web:57]
- **AI policy:** The guidance recommends adopting an AI policy specific to fundraising that explains how AI is used, supports compliance, and may be published for transparency.[web:62][web:58]
- **Transparency:** Donors should not be misled by AI use; not all AI use needs full labelling, but where AI-generated content could reasonably mislead donors, transparency is important.[web:58]
- **Data protection and security:** Charities must understand how AI tools store, use and share data, whether systems are closed-loop, and should review data protection and cybersecurity measures regularly.[web:58][web:66]
- **Human oversight:** The guidance emphasises meaningful human oversight, recommending that AI-generated content and decisions be checked by trained staff for accuracy, fairness and legality before use.[web:58]

The Fundraising Regulator’s own AI policy states that it will use established AI tools, pilot and test them, protect data, avoid using information about protected characteristics, maintain human review, and consider ethical and environmental factors (e.g. energy use, bias, social concerns).[web:66]

### Vulnerable donors, fairness and discrimination

Fundraising-specific AI risks are heightened when donors are vulnerable or when AI systems influence how donors are treated:

- **Vulnerable donors:** AI could increase pressure on individuals if messaging is overly personalised or manipulative, particularly when charities use behavioural nudges without considering vulnerability.[web:62][web:69]
- **Fairness and discrimination:** Profiling and wealth screening may cluster donors by socio-economic or demographic factors, potentially reinforcing inequalities or excluding certain groups from stewardship, recognition or opportunities.[web:70][web:69]

CIOF’s research highlights ethical concerns, including fairness and equity, and notes that most fundraisers are unfamiliar with relevant regulation.[web:72] Legal commentaries stress that any AI use must comply with Equality Act 2010 obligations, avoiding discriminatory outcomes and ensuring that profiling and segmentation do not unlawfully disadvantage individuals or groups.[web:50][web:71]

- **Summary:** Most respondents use AI to support drafting fundraising content for newsletters, emails, reports and social media; 58% use AI for newsletters/emails/reports and 44% for social media.[web:70]

- **Status:** Documented example via narrative round-up.
- **Summary:** A tool such as ProspectAI scans public data for major-donor prospects using AI, summarising donor profiles; this supports fundraisers but raises questions around profiling fairness and transparency.[web:11]

- **Status:** Regulatory policy.
- **Summary:** The Fundraising Regulator’s AI policy describes its own use of AI (trend spotting, service improvement, communications, efficiency) under conditions of piloting, established tools, data protection, no use of protected characteristics, human review and ethical/environmental consideration.[web:66]

## Green, Amber and Red fundraising scenarios

The table below gives indicative ratings; actual classifications depend on specific tools, data and controls.

| Scenario | Rating | Rationale and conditions |
|---------|--------|--------------------------|
| Drafting general fundraising copy from anonymised, approved material, with human editing and fact-checking. | Green | Low risk if no personal data is used and content is reviewed; supports efficiency. |
| Editing and improving existing approved appeals using AI, without adding new claims. | Green | AI as writing aid; human oversight ensures accuracy and tone. |
| Personalising subject lines and messages for segments based on aggregated giving patterns, with clear privacy notices and opt-out. | Amber | Involves profiling but with segment-level data; requires transparency and fairness controls. |
| Using AI to score individual donors and automatically exclude lower-scoring donors from stewardship lists. | Red | Automated decisions about individuals; fairness, discrimination and transparency concerns. |
| Wealth screening using AI from opaque third-party data sources, with limited transparency to donors. | Red | High risk of unfairness and intrusive profiling; requires DPIA and legal review if considered. |
| AI-assisted grant prospecting using public funder data, with human review of eligibility. | Green | Uses public information; main risk is misinterpretation; human review mitigates. |
| AI-generated donor segmentation based on behavioural data, informing but not deciding campaign strategies. | Amber | Profiling; AI supports human decisions; requires DPIA screening and fairness review. |
| Chatbot answering simple fundraising FAQs (e.g. event dates, donation methods), clearly labelled and with human contact available. | Amber | Public-facing; risk of misinformation mitigated by clear boundaries and human support. |
| Chatbot giving personalised fundraising advice based on donor profiles (e.g. suggested donation amount), without human oversight. | Red | Automated personal advice; fairness and pressure risks; potential for harm to vulnerable donors. |

## Implications by charity size

- **Micro and small charities:** Likely to rely on free, consumer AI tools for drafting, editing and grant writing; proportionate governance focuses on safe drafting uses, clear prohibitions on uploading donor or beneficiary data, and simple review processes.[web:65][web:70][web:72]
- **Medium-sized charities:** More likely to explore AI in donor segmentation, automated journeys and prospect research; governance requires AI policies, DPIA screening for profiling and segmentation, and careful review of vendor tools.[web:65][web:68][web:62]
- **Large and complex charities:** May invest in predictive analytics, CRM-integrated AI and advanced segmentation; governance should include full DPIAs, board oversight, fairness testing and explicit policies on vulnerable donors.[web:65][web:70][web:71]

## Practical implications

- AI drafting tools can save time and improve fundraising copy, but human review is essential to ensure accuracy, tone and respect for beneficiaries and donors.
- Profiling, segmentation and wealth screening introduce significant fairness, transparency and legal risks; they require DPIA, clear privacy notices, opt-out mechanisms and human judgement.
- Chatbots and automated journeys must be carefully scoped, tested and monitored to avoid misleading donors or exerting undue pressure.

## Recommended controls

### People and accountability

- Assign fundraising AI leads or include AI risks in existing fundraising governance roles.
- Train fundraisers on AI risks, data protection, ethics and the Fundraising Regulator guidance.

### Policies and processes

- Develop fundraising-specific AI policies and procedures, aligned with organisation-wide AI and data policies.
- Require AI risk assessments (or DPIA screening) for profiling, predictive analytics, wealth screening, chatbots and automated journeys.

### Data protection and transparency

- Ensure lawful basis (often legitimate interests) for AI-enabled profiling; provide clear privacy notices explaining AI use, profiling and opt-out rights.
- Avoid using special-category data or protected characteristics for AI-driven targeting unless strictly justified and lawful.

### Safeguarding and vulnerable donors

- Identify and protect vulnerable donors; avoid AI systems that could intensify pressure or exploit vulnerability.
- Provide clear channels for human support and complaint handling.

### Human review and fairness

- Mandate human review of AI-generated content and decisions before use.
- Conduct fairness and bias audits of segmentation and predictive models, especially where decisions affect donor treatment.

## Proportionate implementation

- **Minimum practice:** Use AI for drafting and editing copy with human review; prohibit donor-level profiling in consumer tools; integrate Fundraising Regulator guidance into policies and training.[web:62][web:58]
- **Stronger practice:** Implement DPIA screening, fairness reviews, transparent privacy notices and controlled CRM-integrated AI for segmentation in medium and large charities.[web:65][web:68]
- **High-risk contexts:** Wealth screening, automated donor treatment decisions and vulnerable-donor interactions require full DPIAs, legal advice and board-level oversight.

## Questions charities should ask

- Are we using AI primarily for drafting and support, or for profiling and decision-making about donors?
- What personal data is processed by our fundraising AI tools, and is our lawful basis and transparency clear?
- How might our AI use affect vulnerable donors or fairness across donor groups?
- What human oversight processes exist, and are they sufficient?

## Evidence gaps and uncertainties

- Limited charity-specific incident data on AI-related harms in fundraising; more research is needed on donor perceptions and long-term effects.
- Limited independent testing of AI fundraising tools and algorithms for fairness and bias.

## Conclusions

AI is now a significant part of UK charity fundraising, particularly in drafting and communications; sector and regulator guidance affirm that AI can offer efficiency and strategic benefits but must be used within the existing legal and ethical framework for fundraising.[web:65][web:70][web:62][web:58] Charities can safely adopt AI for content drafting and grant prospecting with basic controls, but profiling, predictive analytics, wealth screening and chatbots require more robust governance, DPIA, transparency and human oversight, especially where decisions affect vulnerable donors.
