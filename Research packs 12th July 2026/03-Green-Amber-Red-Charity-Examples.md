# Green, Amber and Red Charity AI Examples

## Executive summary

UK charity regulators and sector bodies increasingly emphasise proportionate AI risk assessment and human oversight, but do not themselves provide a formal Green/Amber/Red classification of AI uses.[web:51][web:58][web:48][web:53] This pack therefore develops a practical, evidence-informed Green–Amber–Red framework for charity AI scenarios, drawing on UK guidance, risk registers and governance advice while clearly stating that the ratings are **practical risk guidance**, not regulator-defined categories.[web:45][web:49][web:50][web:52]

Using this framework, the pack provides 50 scannable examples across communications, fundraising, donor management, service delivery, safeguarding, HR, volunteer management, translation, transcription, images, synthetic media, chatbots, connectors, case notes, health and financial information, grant applications and cybersecurity.[web:45][web:48][web:51][web:58] Each example specifies conditions under which a scenario might move from Green to Amber or Red, based on information sensitivity, automation, beneficiary impact, connector scope and human oversight.

## Scope

This pack covers:

- A practical Green/Amber/Red model for charity AI uses.
- 50 example scenarios across key operational areas.
- Risk factors that change the rating (data sensitivity, automation level, beneficiary involvement, connector reach, human review).
- Safeguards and escalation points aligned with UK guidance on AI, data protection, charity governance and fundraising.[web:51][web:48][web:58][web:53]

It does **not** claim to represent official regulatory classifications or legal thresholds. Instead, it synthesises evidence from regulator and sector guidance into practical risk categories that charities can adapt in policy, training and decision-support tools.

## Research questions

1. What types of charity AI use are relatively low risk when implemented with basic controls?
2. Which uses require additional checks, approvals, DPIA screening or specialist input?
3. Which uses are unsuitable in their proposed form because they present unacceptable privacy, safeguarding, legal or trust risks?
4. How do data sensitivity, beneficiary vulnerability, automation level and connector scope affect risk classification?
5. How can a Green/Amber/Red model help charities make proportionate, evidence-led decisions without overstating or understating risk?

## Method

### Source identification

Sources included:

- Charity Commission blog on charities and AI, emphasising trustee duties, accountability and proportionate oversight.[web:51]
- Fundraising Regulator guidance on AI in fundraising, highlighting accountability, risk assessment, policies, transparency and human oversight.[web:58][web:57]
- AMRC briefing on generative AI risks for charities (privacy, hallucination, inappropriate content, bias and regulatory compliance).[web:48]
- Charity Excellence AI risk register and policy templates, which offer practical risk categories and controls.[web:45][web:49][web:47]
- Specialist law firm and sector analyses on AI risks and governance for charities.[web:50][web:52][web:55]
- Wider sector pieces on digital and AI risk, governance codes and trustee responsibilities.[web:53][web:54][web:46]

### Evidence hierarchy and limitations

Regulator guidance (Charity Commission, Fundraising Regulator, ICO references) is treated as primary for risk themes and accountability but does not define specific scenario ratings.[web:51][web:58] Sector tools (Charity Excellence risk registers, trustee AI guides) provide practical risk framing but are not regulatory standards.[web:45][web:49][web:46] Legal commentaries add context on liabilities and compliance but often focus on general principles rather than concrete charity examples.[web:50][web:52]

Where evidence is limited, scenarios are constructed as composite or hypothetical teaching examples, clearly labelled and based on documented risk themes rather than claimed incidents.

## Green–Amber–Red model (practical guidance)

- **Green:** Generally low-risk use involving public, non-sensitive, synthetic or appropriately anonymised information, with proportionate human review. Does **not** mean risk-free or automatically approved.
- **Amber:** Potentially useful but requires additional checks, approval, safeguards, documented assessment or specialist involvement (e.g. data protection, safeguarding, HR).
- **Red:** A use that should **not** proceed in its proposed form because it presents unacceptable privacy, safeguarding, legal, security, discrimination, autonomy or human-impact risks. Red may mean redesigning the activity rather than abandoning the underlying objective.

This model is derived from themes in risk registers, governance advice and sector guidance on proportionate risk management, not from any regulator’s formal classification.[web:45][web:49][web:53][web:58]

## Practical implications

- The Green–Amber–Red framework helps charities quickly identify which AI uses are relatively safe with basic controls and which demand deeper scrutiny, governance and specialist input.
- Ratings are dynamic: the same activity can shift from Green to Amber or Red depending on data sensitivity, connector scope, automation degree, beneficiary involvement and human oversight.
- Regulators emphasise accountability, risk assessment, human review and transparency, rather than specific tools or ratings; charities should treat this framework as a practical lens, not a regulatory checklist.[web:51][web:58][web:48]

## Recommended controls by rating

- **Green uses:** Basic policy coverage, human review, simple documentation, proportionate training; organisational accounts preferred over consumer tools.
- **Amber uses:** Documented risk assessment (or DPIA screening), specialist input (DP, safeguarding, HR), clearer approvals and monitoring; supplier due diligence and connector testing.
- **Red uses:** Do not proceed in the proposed form; redesign objectives to reduce data sensitivity, automation and beneficiary impact; seek legal and specialist advice before considering any alternative.

## Proportionate implementation

- Micro and small charities can use the table to block obviously Red uses (e.g. automated crisis triage, uploading safeguarding notes to public tools) and focus on safe Green uses with simple controls.
- Medium and large charities can embed this framework into AI registers, approval forms and risk registers, linking Amber and Red scenarios to DPIA, board oversight and specialist review.

## Questions charities should ask

- Which rating best fits this proposed AI activity given our data, people and systems?
- What would need to change (data scope, connector design, human review) to move a scenario from Amber or Red towards Green?
- Who needs to be involved (trustees, safeguarding, DP, HR, IT) before we approve or reject this use?

## Evidence gaps and uncertainties

- The framework is based on risk themes rather than extensive charity-specific incident data; ratings may need adjustment as more cases and regulator guidance emerge.
- Some scenarios involve complex legal questions (e.g. automated decisions under UK GDPR) that require dedicated legal advice rather than sector guidance alone.

## Conclusions

The Green–Amber–Red examples provide a practical tool for UK charities to scan proposed AI uses for risk and to decide which activities should be encouraged, scrutinised or stopped. They reflect regulator emphasis on accountability, proportionate risk assessment and human oversight, while recognising that risk depends more on context and data sensitivity than on charity size. Charities should adapt this framework to their own policies, risk appetite and governance structures, and update it as evidence and regulatory guidance evolve.
