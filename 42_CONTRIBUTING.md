# Contributing

Thank you for your interest in contributing to the **AI Safe Use Knowledge Base for UK Charities**.

This project is built to improve through:

- charity experience
- evidence
- research
- specialist review
- public feedback
- practical examples
- corrections
- open-source collaboration

Contributions are welcome from:

- charity staff
- trustees
- volunteers
- researchers
- data-protection professionals
- safeguarding specialists
- digital practitioners
- accessibility specialists
- developers
- AI practitioners
- sector bodies
- funders
- commissioners
- people with lived experience relevant to the guidance

You do not need to be technical to contribute.

---

# Ways to Contribute

You can help by:

- reporting an error
- flagging outdated guidance
- suggesting a source
- asking a question
- suggesting a new topic
- sharing a charity workflow
- proposing a practical example
- reviewing a page
- testing accessibility
- joining user research
- opening a GitHub issue
- proposing a pull request
- improving code or documentation
- translating approved content
- contributing an anonymised case study

---

# Contribution Principles

All contributions should support the project's core principles:

- evidence before opinion
- small charities first
- plain English
- practical guidance
- human judgement
- safeguarding
- data protection
- transparency
- accessibility
- vendor neutrality
- proportionality

Contributions should help charities make better decisions, not simply promote AI adoption.

---

# Before You Contribute

Please check whether:

- the topic already exists
- an issue is already open
- the source is current
- the information applies to UK charities
- the contribution includes personal or confidential information
- specialist review may be required
- the contribution could affect high-risk guidance

For larger changes, open an issue before preparing a pull request.

# Types of Contribution

## Report an Error

Use this for:

- factual mistakes
- broken links
- incorrect source references
- outdated tool guidance
- unclear wording
- missing attribution
- accessibility problems

Please include:

- page URL
- page title
- current page version
- description of the issue
- suggested correction
- supporting source where possible

## Suggest a Source

Use this for:

- new regulator guidance
- charity-sector reports
- official provider documentation
- academic research
- professional guidance
- relevant public charity policies

Please include:

- source title
- organisation or author
- URL
- publication or update date
- why it matters
- pages it may affect

Do not submit pirated or unlawfully shared material.

## Suggest a Topic

Explain:

- the question charities are asking
- the likely audience
- the workflow involved
- why current guidance is insufficient
- whether the issue is Green, Amber or Red
- any relevant evidence

## Share a Charity Workflow

Examples:

- writing a funding bid
- using meeting transcription
- analysing survey responses
- creating a case study
- preparing board papers
- managing volunteers
- reviewing a policy

Do not include real personal data, safeguarding information or confidential organisational details.

## Review a Page

You can review for:

- factual accuracy
- charity relevance
- safeguarding
- data protection
- accessibility
- plain English
- technical correctness
- tool accuracy
- legal clarity
- usability

State your area of expertise and the scope of your review.

## Submit a Pull Request

Pull requests may include:

- new pages
- corrections
- examples
- template improvements
- accessibility fixes
- code changes
- navigation improvements
- validation scripts
- metadata improvements

All pull requests are reviewed before merge.

# Evidence Standards

Material factual claims should use reliable sources.

Preferred order:

1. legislation
2. regulators
3. government
4. official provider documentation
5. recognised charity-sector bodies
6. academic and professional research
7. supporting practice examples

Avoid relying on:

- unsourced claims
- search-result snippets
- social-media posts
- marketing language
- opinion presented as fact
- outdated screenshots
- AI-generated citations

Where evidence is uncertain, say so.

---

# Source Requirements

When adding a source, provide:

- title
- organisation or author
- URL
- publication date
- last-updated date where available
- date accessed
- jurisdiction
- relevant claim
- limitations

The maintainer may assign a Source ID.

---

# Claim Requirements

Important public claims should be:

- specific
- proportionate
- supported
- linked to sources
- limited to what the evidence proves
- reviewed when evidence changes

Do not present recommendations as law.

---

# Editorial Style

Write like:

> A calm, experienced digital colleague helping a charity make a sensible decision.

Use:

- plain English
- direct sentences
- short paragraphs
- charity-specific examples
- practical next steps
- visible uncertainty where needed

Avoid:

- hype
- fearmongering
- corporate jargon
- legalistic copying
- generic AI language
- unnecessary technical terms
- claims that AI will transform everything

---

# Page Structure

Where relevant, follow this order:

1. Quick answer
2. Traffic-light result
3. What to do
4. Example
5. Why it matters
6. Deeper guidance
7. Larger charity considerations
8. Related templates
9. Sources
10. Feedback

---

# Green, Amber and Red

Use:

- **Green — Go ahead with normal checks**
- **Amber — Pause and check**
- **Red — Stop**

Do not rely on colour alone.

The rating applies to the task and context, not to the provider brand as a whole.

---

# Privacy and Safeguarding

Never contribute:

- safeguarding case information
- identifiable beneficiary information
- donor records
- employee records
- health information
- personal contact details
- passwords
- API keys
- confidential contracts
- privileged legal advice
- raw survey responses containing identifiers
- interview transcripts containing personal data

Use:

- fictional examples
- aggregated findings
- anonymised summaries
- synthetic data
- public information

If you are unsure, leave the information out and ask first.

---

# Research Contributions

Research contributions may include:

- aggregated survey findings
- anonymised interview themes
- usability findings
- search trends
- sector evidence
- public case studies

Please include:

- research question
- method
- sample
- date range
- limitations
- consent and privacy approach
- how findings were analysed

Do not present a small or self-selecting sample as representative of the entire charity sector.

---

# Case Studies

A case study should explain:

- the charity context
- the problem
- the AI use
- the data involved
- the controls used
- the outcome
- lessons learned
- limitations

Use fictional or anonymised details unless there is explicit permission to publish identifiable information.

---

# Tool Reviews

Tool contributions must specify:

- product
- plan
- account type
- region where relevant
- documentation reviewed
- review date
- suitable uses
- limitations
- privacy considerations
- governance considerations

Do not assume free, business and enterprise plans have the same safeguards.

Do not submit promotional copy as an independent review.

# Technical Contributions

Technical changes should:

- keep the site Markdown-first
- avoid unnecessary dependencies
- preserve accessibility
- preserve stable URLs
- include validation
- pass the site build
- include clear documentation
- avoid exposing secrets
- remain maintainable by a small team

For significant technical changes, open an issue first.

---

# Branch and Pull Request Workflow

Recommended:

1. Fork or branch the repository.
2. Create a focused branch.
3. Make one coherent change.
4. Run validation.
5. Preview the site.
6. Update tests where relevant.
7. Open a pull request.
8. Respond to review.
9. Revise if needed.
10. Merge after approval.

Suggested branch names:

```text
docs/add-volunteer-ai-guidance
fix/correct-ico-link
accessibility/improve-table-navigation
research/add-2026-survey-findings
```

---

# Pull Request Requirements

Every pull request should include:

- summary
- reason for change
- pages affected
- evidence reviewed
- Source IDs where known
- claims affected
- screenshots for visual changes
- validation completed
- known limitations
- specialist review needs

Do not mix unrelated changes into one pull request.

---

# Commit Messages

Use clear messages.

Good:

```text
Add volunteer AI-use guidance
Correct special-category-data example
Update Claude privacy review date
Improve keyboard focus in decision tree
```

Avoid:

```text
changes
updates
fixes
final
```

# Review Process

A contribution may be reviewed for:

- evidence
- legal wording
- safeguarding
- charity relevance
- accessibility
- editorial style
- technical quality
- privacy
- licensing
- duplication
- navigation impact

High-risk guidance may require specialist review.

Not every contribution will be accepted.

A contribution may be:

- accepted
- revised
- deferred
- partially accepted
- closed with explanation
- rejected where it does not meet project standards

---

# Human Approval

Claude or another AI tool may assist with:

- drafting
- summarising
- editing
- code generation
- research comparison

AI-generated contributions must still be:

- checked
- attributed where required
- reviewed for accuracy
- reviewed for privacy
- reviewed for copyright
- approved by a human

Do not submit AI-generated citations without verifying them.

# Copyright and Licensing

Only contribute material you have the right to share.

Do not submit:

- copied books
- paid reports
- restricted training material
- long extracts
- copyrighted templates without permission
- proprietary code without permission

By contributing, you agree that accepted contributions may be published under the project's chosen licence.

Contributor licensing terms should be confirmed before public launch.
