    2026 Evidence Snapshot¶

    This page distils the July 2026 research packs into public-facing findings for UK charities. It is a synthesis, not a replacement for legal, safeguarding, data-protection or procurement advice.

    What Changed¶

    Adoption AI is now ordinary workplace behaviour Sector surveys indicate widespread charity use of AI, especially for drafting, bids, communications, research and administration.

    Governance Oversight is lagging behind use Many charities still lack a clear AI policy, use register, approval process, trustee dashboard or incident route.

    Trust Public acceptance is conditional People are more comfortable with back-office AI than with AI influencing support, eligibility, safeguarding or sensitive personal data.

    Twenty Findings¶

    Adoption and Governance¶

    1. AI use is now mainstream across UK charities, with reported use concentrated in drafting, research, fundraising, administration and content work.
    2. Governance has not kept pace. The priority is not simply "more AI", but safer AI: policies, registers, training, DPIAs, supplier checks and trustee oversight.
    3. Trustees may underestimate actual AI use because staff and volunteers often experiment through consumer accounts before formal approval.
    4. Shadow AI is a major risk route. Consumer tools can bypass organisational contracts, retention settings, training controls and audit logs.

    Public Trust and Beneficiaries¶

    1. Public attitudes are cautious and conditional. Trust falls when AI influences decisions about who receives help or when sensitive personal data is involved.
    2. Charities should be transparent when AI materially shapes public content, donor communications or service journeys.
    3. General-purpose chatbots are unsafe substitutes for crisis support, safeguarding, clinical, legal or benefits advice.
    4. Synthetic images and synthetic beneficiary stories can damage trust if audiences may confuse them with real people or real events.

    Risk Patterns¶

    1. Hallucinated facts, fabricated citations and confident misinformation remain core failure modes.
    2. Health, mental-health, safeguarding and legal contexts require human professional judgement and verified sources.
    3. Bias and discrimination risks are well documented in public-sector algorithmic decision-making and are directly relevant to charity profiling, triage and eligibility.
    4. Charity-specific AI incident evidence is still sparse, so near-miss learning should be deliberately collected.

    Regulation and Oversight¶

    1. UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018 and PECR apply to AI-enabled charity activities where relevant.
    2. Charity Commission expectations around trustee duties still apply when AI is used.
    3. The Fundraising Regulator expects charitable fundraising institutions to remain responsible and accountable for AI-generated outputs they use.
    4. AI governance should sit inside existing risk, safeguarding, data-protection, procurement and incident processes.

    Tools and Connectors¶

    1. Enterprise AI tools can offer stronger controls than consumer accounts, but no plan is automatically safe for every charity use.
    2. Connectors to SharePoint, Drive, Slack, email or CRMs are a critical risk point because they can expose existing permission problems at scale.
    3. "Not used for training" is useful but incomplete. Charities still need to check retention, deletion, subprocessors, human access, audit logs, DPA terms and connector scope.
    4. The practical route is proportionate: Green uses with normal review, Amber uses with documented checks, and Red uses redesigned or stopped.

    Implications for This Site¶

    The July 2026 research strengthens the site's existing direction:

    • keep the Before You Paste rule simple and prominent
    • treat beneficiary-facing AI, donor profiling, recruitment scoring and autonomous agents as high-risk
    • make account-plan and connector checks part of tool approval
    • give trustees a one-page assurance view
    • invite contributors to improve the evidence base with clear source standards
