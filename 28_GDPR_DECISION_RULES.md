# Purpose

This document defines the decision rules used throughout the knowledge base whenever AI is used with information that may be protected by UK GDPR or related legislation.

It powers:

- Before You Paste
- Green / Amber / Red decisions
- workflow guidance
- templates
- AI tool reviews
- policy templates

It is guidance based on current UK regulatory expectations and is not legal advice.

# Core Principle

> Use the minimum information needed, protect people before prompts, and keep meaningful human judgement.

# Decision Sequence

Always ask these questions in order.

## Rule 1 — Is AI needed?

Could the task be completed without AI?

If yes, consider whether AI adds genuine value.

## Rule 2 — Is any information personal data?

Personal data includes any information relating to an identified or identifiable person.

Examples:

Green:
- public statistics
- fictional people
- generic text

Red:
- names
- email addresses
- phone numbers
- supporter records
- volunteer records
- employee records

## Rule 3 — Is it special category data?

Examples include:

- health
- ethnicity
- religion
- political opinions
- trade union membership
- sexual orientation
- biometric data
- genetic data

If yes:

Default classification = RED unless processed within an approved governance framework.

## Rule 4 — Does it involve safeguarding?

Examples:

- abuse disclosures
- risk assessments
- children
- vulnerable adults
- domestic abuse
- suicide risk
- criminal allegations

Default classification = RED.

## Rule 5 — Is the information confidential?

Examples:

- board papers
- legal advice
- contracts
- disciplinary records
- unpublished financial information

Usually AMBER or RED depending on the tool and controls.

## Rule 6 — Can the information be anonymised?

Prefer:

- aggregation
- pseudonymisation
- fictional examples
- synthetic datasets

If anonymisation removes the risk, reassess.

## Rule 7 — Is the AI tool approved?

Consider:

- business or enterprise account
- privacy controls
- data retention
- contractual protections
- organisational approval

Personal free accounts should not be assumed to be appropriate for organisational information.

## Rule 8 — Could the output affect a person?

If AI may influence:

- eligibility
- funding
- recruitment
- safeguarding
- disciplinary action
- legal rights

Require meaningful human oversight.

## Rule 9 — Would you be comfortable explaining this use?

Ask:

- Would I explain this to the individual concerned?
- Would I explain it to trustees?
- Would I explain it to the ICO?

If not, stop and review.

# Traffic Light Rules

## Green

Suitable examples:

- brainstorming
- drafting from public information
- grammar
- translation
- formatting
- fictional case studies
- public policy summaries

## Amber

Examples:

- anonymised internal reports
- internal planning
- aggregated survey themes
- confidential but non-personal information

Requires:

- approved tool
- human review
- manager judgement

## Red

Never place into public AI tools:

- identifiable beneficiary information
- safeguarding records
- donor databases
- payroll
- HR case files
- medical information
- passwords
- API keys
- banking information
- legal privilege
- criminal allegations

# DPIA Indicators

A Data Protection Impact Assessment may be appropriate when:

- high-risk processing is planned
- large volumes of personal data are involved
- vulnerable people are affected
- profiling or automated decision-making is introduced
- new technology creates significant privacy risks

Refer to organisational governance and ICO guidance.

# Human Review Rules

AI must not become the final decision maker for:

- safeguarding
- employment
- trustee decisions
- legal advice
- funding eligibility
- complaints
- disciplinary action

# Golden Rules

1. Protect people before prompts.
2. Use the least data possible.
3. Prefer anonymisation.
4. Keep humans accountable.
5. Check facts before publishing.
6. Record important AI use.
7. Escalate uncertainty.
8. When in doubt, leave it out.
