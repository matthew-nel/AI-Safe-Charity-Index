# Purpose

This document defines the safeguarding principles that apply whenever AI is used by charities working with children, young people, families or adults at risk.

Safeguarding guidance sits alongside GDPR. They overlap, but they are not the same.

The first priority is always protecting people from harm.

> **Protect people before prompts.**

This guidance is not legal advice and does not replace organisational safeguarding policies or statutory guidance.

---

# Objectives

The knowledge base should help charities:

- recognise safeguarding risks before using AI
- understand when AI is appropriate
- understand when AI should not be used
- reduce accidental disclosure
- keep human judgement central
- protect trust and dignity

---

# Core Principles

1. Human safety comes first.
2. AI supports people—it does not replace professional judgement.
3. Safeguarding decisions must never be delegated to AI.
4. Use the minimum information necessary.
5. Prefer fictional or anonymised examples.
6. Escalate uncertainty to an appropriate safeguarding lead.

---

# High-Risk Information

Never place the following into public AI systems unless organisational governance explicitly permits it and appropriate safeguards exist.

Examples include:

- safeguarding referrals
- abuse disclosures
- child protection information
- domestic abuse case notes
- sexual abuse disclosures
- mental health crisis records
- suicide risk information
- social care assessments
- identifiable family histories
- police information
- court information
- health records
- foster care information

Default classification:

**RED**

---

# Appropriate AI Uses

Generally suitable:

- drafting generic safeguarding policies
- creating fictional training scenarios
- improving plain English
- summarising public safeguarding guidance
- brainstorming awareness campaigns
- creating staff training quizzes

Human review is always required.

---

# Caution Areas

Treat carefully:

- anonymised safeguarding themes
- aggregated incident trends
- internal learning reports
- safeguarding training design
- supervision guidance

These usually fall into **AMBER**.

---

# Never Use AI To

- determine whether abuse has occurred
- assess immediate risk
- decide whether to refer a concern
- replace safeguarding supervision
- replace professional advice
- replace legal advice
- generate facts that are not known
- create fictional evidence

---

# Human Oversight

Meaningful human review must remain with trained staff.

AI may assist with:

- drafting
- formatting
- summarising
- structuring

AI must not become the decision maker.

---

# Safeguarding Questions

Before using AI ask:

- Could someone be identified?
- Could harm result from an error?
- Does this involve a child?
- Does this involve an adult at risk?
- Does this involve abuse?
- Could this affect somebody's safety?
- Can the information be anonymised?
- Does the safeguarding lead need to be involved?

If unsure:

Stop.

# Charity Examples

## Green

Create a fictional safeguarding training exercise.

## Amber

Summarise anonymised themes from safeguarding audits.

## Red

Paste a real safeguarding referral into ChatGPT.

---

# Relationship to GDPR

GDPR focuses on lawful processing.

Safeguarding focuses on protecting people.

Some activities engage both.

Always satisfy safeguarding requirements first.

---

# Escalation

Escalate to:

- Safeguarding Lead
- Data Protection Lead
- Senior Manager
- Trustee responsible for safeguarding

where appropriate.

# Review Triggers

Review immediately following:

- safeguarding law changes
- Charity Commission updates
- ICO updates affecting vulnerable people
- major safeguarding incidents
- new AI capabilities
- repeated charity feedback

---

# Monthly Claude Prompt

Review safeguarding guidance against:

- regulator updates
- safeguarding organisations
- charity feedback
- new AI tools

Identify:

- pages requiring updates
- workflow changes
- new examples
- high-risk guidance requiring review

Never weaken safeguarding protections to accommodate new technology.

---

# Success

This document succeeds when charities understand that AI can support safeguarding practice but must never replace safeguarding responsibility, professional judgement or accountability.
